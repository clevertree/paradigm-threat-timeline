# The Length of a Year Changes Throughout Antiquity

Earth's orbit changed over time — from the collinear Saturnian configuration (Golden Age) through electromagnetic close-encounters with Venus and Mars, into its final stable orbit. With each *jump*, calendar year-length changed dramatically and ancient cultures reformed their calendars to match.

Time-keeping became possible at **4,077 BC**, when Saturn was electrically ignited and began to visibly turn in Earth's sky. No surviving calendar predates this because *no time was being reckoned*.

[Jno Cook](https://www.yumpu.com/en/document/view/54821831/saturnian-cosmology-appendix-a-notes-on-chronology) writes:

> The whole idea of even suggesting that the length of the year could have changed in the past comes from the solidly established changes seen in the 8th century BCE, when worldwide calendars of 360 days were superseded by calendars of 365 days plus one quarter day.

## Era Highlights

**225-day year (Golden Age, 4077–3147 BC):** Earth at ~0.72 AU. No Moon. Sumerian King Lists' pre-flood reign-lengths are consistent only with 225-day years.

**260-day year (Fall of the Absu, 2349–2193 BC):** The **Mesoamerican Tzolkin** (260-day ritual calendar, still in use) preserves this era's year-length exactly.

**273-day year (Middle Kingdom, 2193–1492 BC):** Human gestation (~273 days) may preserve a calibration memory.

**360-day year (New Kingdom, 1492–747 BC):** Most documented era. **The 360 degrees of circular measure derives from this period.** Babylon, Egypt, Maya Long Count, India, China, and Rome all record the same structure.

**365.24-day year (747 BC–present):** Final jump ~Feb 27, 747 BC (Era of Nabonassar). Five epagomenal days added worldwide.

## Carbon-14 Corroboration

Cook compared dates using progressively shorter years against the **INTCAL98 radiocarbon calibration table** and found strong agreement back to ~4077 BC. The C-14 curve's "wiggles" align with year-length boundary events — independent corroboration of Earth being closer to the Sun in ancient times.
