History as presented today resembles a tumbleweed — a tangled mass gathering layers of complexity with each tumble, so knotted that no one can trace a single thread back to where it started. That complexity is treated as inherent, as though the past is simply too messy for ordinary people to grasp. But history should look like a tree: a clear foundation, a trunk you can follow, and branches that diverge in ways anyone could understand at a single glance. The tumbleweed is not the natural shape of history — it is the shape of history after centuries of deliberate cutting, grafting, and tangling by those who benefit from the confusion. How do we get back to the tree? I started this timeline project because I had faith since I was young that technology can solve any human problem. 

I was born and raised Jewish. The faith and history were hard for me to accept — things didn't add up. I was drawn to the emerging technologies of the 1980s: computers and software, which *did* add up. They were causal, logical, honest. Technology became my religion. But the early 1990s optimism — the internet decentralizing knowledge, software solving the big problems — faded year by year. The technology I believed in was systematically captured: surveillance platforms replaced open protocols, artificial scarcity replaced abundance. By the 2010s the dystopia was undeniable.

Then I noticed the same pattern in history — the same cycles of war, oppression, and collapse repeating across centuries, as though the human species were either unwilling or unable to learn from the horrors of the past. The implication was that we had a greedy, warlike nature that could not be mitigated without some sort of unified world order. Once I started looking, the pattern appeared in nearly every other field too: medicine, energy, education, agriculture — wherever a technology threatened to decentralize power, it was captured or suppressed.

If history was written by the victors, then centuries of warfare produced a highly redacted, winner's-eye view — scrubbed, compressed, and reordered to serve those who controlled record-keeping.

As a software developer, I'm wired toward causality. I want to know *why* something works the way it does, and trace the chain back to its origin. In software, there are no biases — code either runs or it doesn't. Cause follows effect.

Applying that principle to history — using modern software tools, especially AI agents to OCR old books in multiple languages and cross-reference conclusions — I set out to reconstruct a potentially highly redacted history of Earth. This timeline is the result.

What pushed me in this direction was censorship. Around 2010, I noticed a flourishing of controversial ideas on Facebook, Google+, and the early social web — everything from Hollow Earth to unorthodox WW2 history to suppressed science. Then, within a year or two, all of it vanished. Not organically. Systematically. Shadowbanned, permabanned, demonetized, downvoted, downranked — every mechanism available was deployed against specific topics with striking consistency. I started collecting those materials before they disappeared, building an archive I eventually released as the **Paradigm Threat Repository**.

The aggression of that censorship was, paradoxically, the most clarifying signal I received. When a topic is ignored, it may simply be fringe. When a topic is *actively suppressed* with coordinated effort across every major platform simultaneously, it means something else. The blatancy of it points in one direction: the "first story" (mainstream consensus) and the "second story" (conspiracy counter-narrative) are both managed — a divide-and-conquer structure designed to exhaust us in arguments between two controlled positions, preventing us from perceiving a third story altogether.

## The Paradigm Threat

This project exists to pose an **intellectual challenge** to the reigning paradigms of every discipline — history, cosmology, medicine, physics, religion, finance, governance fortified with sourced research, cross-referenced chronologies, AI Agent analysis, and a willingness to follow the evidence wherever it leads.

Every field has its orthodoxy. Every orthodoxy has its gatekeepers. And every gatekeeper benefits from the assumption that the paradigm they protect is settled, unassailable, not worth re-examining. **We disagree.** If a paradigm cannot survive scrutiny, it does not deserve the authority it claims. If it *can* survive scrutiny, then the challenge only makes it stronger. Either way, the challenge is the point.

This project is armed with history and ready to debate. We invite scholars, skeptics, and anyone willing to think critically to engage with the material on its merits. We are not asking anyone to believe — we are asking everyone to *look*.
