# 1918–1991 CE — Soviet Historiography: Final Layer

## The Construction of Official History

After the revolution, the Soviet state did not merely censor history — it **manufactured** it. Party-approved historians rewrote the entire Russian past according to Marxist periodization: primitive communism → feudalism → capitalism → socialism. Every event was forced into this schema; events that did not fit were eliminated. The Marxist stages were not a theory to be tested — they were a **template** imposed.

## The Destruction of Archives

**Churches and Monasteries:** ~50,000 destroyed or repurposed (1917–1941). Monastic libraries — the oldest Slavic manuscript repositories — dispersed, many destroyed. The Solovetsky Monastery, holding one of Russia's largest manuscript collections, became a labour camp (1923).

**Aristocratic Archives:** Estate libraries nationalized, many destroyed during Civil War chaos. Family papers, genealogies, and correspondence burned or lost.

**Village Records:** Forced collectivization destroyed the *obshchina* communes and scattered their members. By 1940, the organic village structure preserving local memory for centuries was gone.

**Academic Archives:** Universities purged of "bourgeois" scholars. Research contradicting Marxist orthodoxy suppressed. Nikolai Morozov — whose chronological research anticipated Fomenko's — was released from prison only to see his work marginalized.

## Mikhail Pokrovsky and the Marxist School

The key figure was Mikhail Pokrovsky (1868–1932), de facto head of Soviet historical science from 1918 until his death: Russian history understood entirely through class struggle. No great men, no spiritual forces — only economic classes grinding against each other.

After Pokrovsky's death, Stalin paradoxically rehabilitated certain nationalist themes (Ivan the Terrible as hero, Alexander Nevsky as patriot), but the underlying Marxist framework remained. The Stalinist correction was not a return to truth — it was a **second falsification layer** atop Pokrovsky's, which itself sat atop the Romanov layer.

## Ivan Kinkel and the Road Not Taken

Against this backdrop, Kinkel's theory — with biological metaphors, cyclical patterns, and insistence on interconnecting economic, spiritual, and cultural life — represents the **road not taken**. Had a different regime emerged, scholars like Kinkel might have provided a framework neither Marxist nor Scaligerian. Instead, his work was suppressed, his books removed from Bulgarian libraries after 1944, his ideas mocked as "kinkeliads."

## The Layers of Falsification

From the NC perspective, Russian history has endured at least four layers:

1. **Scaligerian (16th–17th C):** False long chronology by Scaliger and Petavius, adopted by Romanovs for legitimacy
2. **Romanov (17th–19th C):** Destruction of Hordian documents; German historians imported to construct "proper" Russian history
3. **Pokrovskian/Marxist (1918–1932):** All Russian history rewritten as class-struggle narrative
4. **Stalinist (1932–1953):** Selective nationalist rehabilitation within the Marxist framework

Each layer was applied by a regime with different motives but converging effect: the deeper Hordian past became progressively harder to recover. Soviet historiography was not the origin but the **final coat of paint** on a 300-year structure — sealing everything beneath, because the Soviet regime had totalitarian apparatus to enforce a single version across a continent for seventy years.

## The Aftermath

When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, archives partially opened. Fomenko's New Chronology, developing quietly since the 1970s, reached a wider audience. But three generations had been educated within the Marxist-Scaligerian framework. Primary sources were destroyed or dispersed. What remains is reconstruction from fragments — precisely what the New Chronology attempts.
