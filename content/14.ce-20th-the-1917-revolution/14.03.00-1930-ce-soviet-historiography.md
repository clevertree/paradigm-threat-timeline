# Soviet Historiography: The Final Falsification Layer

## The Construction of Official History

After the revolution, the Soviet state did not merely censor history — it **manufactured** it. Party-approved historians rewrote the entire Russian past according to Marxist periodisation: primitive communism → feudalism → capitalism → socialism. Every event was forced into this schema. Events that did not fit were eliminated. The Marxist stages were not a theory to be tested — they were a **template** to be imposed.

## The Destruction of Archives

**Churches and Monasteries:** ~50,000 churches destroyed or repurposed (1917–1941). Monastic libraries — the oldest Slavic manuscript repositories — dispersed, many destroyed. The Solovetsky Monastery, holding one of Russia's largest manuscript collections, was converted to a labour camp (1923).

**Aristocratic Archives:** Estate libraries nationalised, many destroyed during Civil War chaos. Family papers, genealogies, and centuries of correspondence burned or lost.

**Village Records:** The *obshchina* maintained oral traditions and local records. Forced collectivisation destroyed the communes and scattered their members. By 1940, the organic village structure that preserved local memory for centuries was gone.

**Academic Archives:** Universities purged of "bourgeois" scholars. Research contradicting Marxist orthodoxy suppressed. Nikolai Morozov — whose chronological research anticipated Fomenko's — was released from prison only to see his work marginalised.

## Mikhail Pokrovsky and the Marxist School

The key figure was Mikhail Pokrovsky (1868–1932), de facto head of Soviet historical science from 1918 until his death. His approach: Russian history understood entirely through class struggle. No great men, no spiritual forces — only the grinding of economic classes.

After Pokrovsky's death, Stalin paradoxically rehabilitated certain nationalist themes (Ivan the Terrible as hero, Alexander Nevsky as patriot), but the underlying Marxist framework remained. The Stalinist correction was not a return to truth — it was a **second layer of falsification** applied on top of Pokrovsky's, which was itself on top of the Romanov layer.

## Ivan Kinkel and the Road Not Taken

Against this backdrop, Kinkel's theory of civilisational development — with biological metaphors, cyclical patterns, and insistence on interconnection of economic, spiritual, and cultural life — represents the **road not taken**. Had a different regime emerged, scholars like Kinkel might have provided a framework that was neither Marxist nor Scaligerian, but rooted in organic patterns Russia's experience exemplified. Instead, his work was suppressed for half a century, his books removed from Bulgarian libraries after 1944, his ideas mocked as "kinkeliads."

## The Layers of Falsification

From the NC perspective, Russian history has endured at least four layers:

1. **Scaligerian (16th–17th C):** False long chronology by Scaliger and Petavius, adopted by Romanovs for legitimacy
2. **Romanov (17th–19th C):** Destruction of Hordian documents; German historians imported to construct "proper" Russian history
3. **Pokrovskian/Marxist (1918–1932):** All Russian history rewritten as Marxist class-struggle narrative
4. **Stalinist (1932–1953):** Selective nationalist rehabilitation within the Marxist framework

Each layer was applied by a regime with different motives but converging effects: the deeper past — the Hordian past — became progressively harder to recover. Soviet historiography was not the origin. It was the **final coat of paint** on a 300-year structure — but the coat that sealed everything beneath, because the Soviet regime had the totalitarian apparatus to enforce a single version of history across a continent for seventy years.

## The Aftermath

When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, archives partially opened. Fomenko's New Chronology, developing quietly since the 1970s, reached a wider audience. But three generations had been educated within the Marxist-Scaligerian framework. Primary sources were destroyed or dispersed. What remains is reconstruction from fragments — which is precisely what the New Chronology attempts.
