# Aftermath of 1812: Russian Pursuit to Paris

Following Napoleon's catastrophic retreat from Moscow, the Russian army did not stop at the border. Under Czar Alexander I, Russian forces pursued the remnants of the Grande Armée westward across Europe, reaching **Paris itself in March 1814**. Russian troops occupied parts of France — Cossack cavalry camped on the Champs-Élysées. The occupation lasted until 1818 under the terms of the [Congress of Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna). It was the farthest west a Russian army would march until 1945.

## Russian Mountains: What the French Found

One cultural artifact the French encountered in Russia were the **"Russian Mountains"** (*Montagnes Russes*) — elaborate ice slides that Russians had been building since at least the 17th century. These were massive wooden-framed ramps coated in ice, reaching heights of 20–25 meters, down which riders would hurtle on sleds at tremendous speed. St. Petersburg had permanent installations; traveling versions appeared at winter festivals across the empire.

The French were astonished. They had nothing comparable. After the occupation and the returning soldiers' accounts, French engineers began building wheeled versions in Paris — the direct ancestor of the modern [roller coaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roller_coaster). To this day, roller coasters in many languages are still called *"Russian Mountains"* (*Montagnes Russes* in French, *Montaña Rusa* in Spanish, *Montagne Russe* in Italian).

The standard history dates Russian ice slides to the 17th century. But given the Hordian Empire's engineering capabilities — the same civilization that built the structures now attributed to "ancient Rome" — these installations likely existed for far longer in the east, part of a winter recreation culture that the western invaders had never encountered because they had never penetrated this deep into Hordian territory. Napoleon's armies almost certainly destroyed every installation they found, as they destroyed everything else. The fact that the tradition survived and was transmitted westward testifies to its deep roots.
