# Napoleon invades Russia

[Main article: The MudFlood and World Cataclysm](11.270-the-mudflood-and-world-cataclysm.md)

Napoleon's invasion of Russia in 1812 — the [Grande Armée](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grande_Arm%C3%A9e)'s catastrophic six-month campaign across the same territories that had been Moscow Tartary just forty years earlier — is one of the most consequential military events in European history. The conventional narrative presents it as Napoleon's fatal overreach: an army of 680,000 that entered Russia and returned as fewer than 100,000 survivors.

## Why Moscow?

The invasion's strategic objective was forcing Czar Alexander I into negotiations by seizing the old symbolic capital. But Moscow in 1812 was no longer the center it had been. The Romanovs had relocated to St. Petersburg; Moscow was an administrative backwater. When Alexander refused to negotiate and the Russians burned Moscow themselves rather than let it serve as French winter headquarters, Napoleon had no strategic objective left. He occupied charred ruins for five weeks and retreated into the Russian winter.

In the NC framework, this has an additional dimension. The territories of former Moscow Tartary — Siberia, the Urals, the far east — had been incorporated into Romanov Russia only since the late 18th century. Local loyalty to the Romanov state was thin. A French-style liberation campaign promising feudal abolition might have found substantial sympathies among recently-conquered Hordian populations. The Romanov court was aware of this — explaining the ferocity of the scorched-earth response, designed to ensure Napoleon found no resources and no allies in the interior.

## The Battle of Borodino and Its Aftermath

The [Battle of Borodino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Borodino) (7 September 1812) was the largest single day's battle in the Napoleonic Wars — 70,000 casualties in one day. Neither side achieved a decisive outcome. The Russian army retreated but maintained cohesion. Napoleon occupied Moscow two weeks later. The subsequent French retreat became one of history's most devastating military disasters: cold, starvation, partisan attacks, and river crossings destroyed the army as an effective force.

## Napoleon and the Hordian Legacy

Tchaikovsky's [1812 Overture](11.310-tchaikovsky-releases-1812-overture.md), composed in 1880, celebrates the Russian victory. It opens with the Russian Orthodox hymn *God Preserve Thy People* (associated with the old Kievan/Hordian church tradition), transitions through French and Russian military themes, and culminates in the *Solemn March* with cannon fire and church bells. The NC reading sees the last musical celebration of the Hordian legacy: Orthodox bells, Old Russian hymns, and cannon fire all belonging to the cultural sphere the Romanovs had been systematically erasing since 1613 — temporarily recovered in the national trauma of 1812.

Napoleon's defeat did not restore the old order; it accelerated the financial one. With Napoleon gone, the Romanovs dominated the Eurasian heartland while the City of London dominated global finance. The stage was set for the 19th century's consolidation of the British Empire and the railroad-and-debt penetration of every remaining independent territory.
