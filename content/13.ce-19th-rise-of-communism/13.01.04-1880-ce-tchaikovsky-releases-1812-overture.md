# Tchaikovsky releases 1812 Overture

Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky composed the [1812 Overture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1812_Overture) in 1880, commissioned for the consecration of the Cathedral of Christ the Saviour in Moscow. Its premiere featured live cannon fire, church bells, and massed military bands.

## Tchaikovsky Hated It

Tchaikovsky privately despised the 1812 Overture. In a letter to his patroness [Nadezhda von Meck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nadezhda_von_Meck), he wrote:

> *"The overture will be very loud and noisy, but I wrote it with no warm feeling of love, and therefore there will probably be no artistic merits in it."*

He later called it "not at all good" and expressed embarrassment at its popularity. This raises a question the conventional narrative cannot answer: why would a composer of Tchaikovsky's stature accept a commission he found artistically repugnant?

The theory: Tchaikovsky was **ordered** to write it. By 1880, France was under the [Third Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Third_Republic), successor to the [Second French Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_French_Empire) of Napoleon III. The Franco-Russian alliance was being carefully cultivated — it would be formalised in 1894. Leaders aligned with French interests needed a grand public narrative of Russian-French reconciliation: a musical monument to 1812 that framed the war as a completed chapter rather than an ongoing civilisational conflict. Tchaikovsky, dependent on state patronage, had no ability to refuse. He did the minimum: produced a technically competent crowd-pleaser with no personal investment, and said so privately.

## The Orthodox Hymn vs. the Marseillaise

The overture opens with **the Russian Orthodox hymn** *God Preserve Thy People*, drawn from the ancient Kievan liturgical tradition — the music of the old Hordian spiritual world. This is pitted against the *Marseillaise* (French Revolutionary anthem) and overwhelmed by cannon blasts and bells. The thematic contest is between the old Orthodox-Imperial order and the new Financial-Republican order Napoleon carried across Europe.

## Tchaikovsky's Musical Testament

Tchaikovsky composed the overture sixty-eight years after the event. By 1880, the old Hordian world was visible only in the Orthodox Church's liturgical music — the same music opening his overture. The cannon fire ending it represents both the Russian victory of 1812 and the gunpowder used to demolish Cathar castles, Hordian fortifications, and every state refusing the new financial order. In the NC reading, the 1812 Overture is the last great piece of *counter-historical* art: a musical argument that the old world was worth fighting for. By the time Tchaikovsky wrote it, that argument had already been lost.
