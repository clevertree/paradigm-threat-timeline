# 1860–1917 CE — 19th Century Assault on Hordian Communalism

## The Obshchina: Living Memory of Hordian Communalism

The Russian village commune (*obshchina*) — with collective land ownership, periodic strip redistribution, and communal decision-making through the *mir* (village assembly) — was the last living institutional memory of how the Hordian world-empire organised its economy. These were not "primitive" structures but degraded yet functional remnants of the system described in the New Chronology: an empire governed through Cossack-military communes with collective land tenure, shared resources, and hereditary obligations.

The 19th-century Russian intelligentsia was obsessed with the *obshchina* question. Slavophiles saw proof of Russia's unique spiritual destiny. Westernisers saw a backward relic. Marxists dismissed it as "primitive communism" to be destroyed so capitalism could develop before the *real* revolution.

The genius of the Marxist demolition manual: it convinced Russian communists to destroy the very institution embodying *actual* communism — the living communal structures inherited from the pre-Romanov era — in the name of a theoretical communism that had never existed.

## Rewriting Russian Economic History

Throughout the 19th century, the Romanov regime systematically rewrote Russian economic history, presenting the pre-Romanov period as "Tatar yoke" and barbarism — obscuring that the so-called Tatar-Mongol Empire was actually the Russian Horde with its own sophisticated economic system.

Kinkel's model provides the framework. The Hordian economic system likely represented an advanced **Form 1** (communal-collectivist) within **Cycle III** (West and East European era), combining:

- Collective land tenure (the *obshchina*)
- Military-administrative governance (the Cossack hosts)
- Communal taxation and resource-sharing
- Integration of spiritual life with economic organisation

The Marxist insistence that this system was "primitive" and needed destruction was ideological justification for demolishing the last remnant of Hordian civilisation.

## The Narodniki: The Road Not Taken

The Narodniks (*народники*) of the 1860s–1880s recognised what Marx refused to admit: Russia's communal institutions might offer a direct path to socialism without passing through full capitalist industrialisation. Chernyshevsky, Herzen, and others argued the *obshchina* was not a relic but a *foundation*.

Marx himself was forced to engage in his 1881 letter to Vera Zasulich, grudgingly admitting the Russian commune *might* serve as "the fulcrum for social regeneration in Russia" — a concession contradicting his entire framework, suppressed by followers after his death.

The Narodniks were crushed — infiltrated, discredited, and replaced by Marxist Social Democracy, which insisted capitalism must develop fully before revolution. The Bolsheviks followed this script: destroyed the communes, collectivised agriculture by force, and murdered millions of peasants in the name of "modernisation."

## Kinkel's Framework Applied

Kinkel explains 19th-century Russia as a transition crisis within Cycle III. The old Hordian system (advanced Form 1) was violently disrupted by the Romanov seizure and Western intervention. The 19th-century conflict between communalists and capitalists was the chaotic process of an imposed transition from Form 1 through Forms 2–3 (petty-individual and capitalist) under external pressure.

Marx's role was providing the narrative making this violent transition appear *inevitable* — natural law rather than political choice by identifiable actors. Kinkel showed transitions between forms are shaped by the *full spectrum* of human factors and the outcome is never predetermined. The destruction of Russia's communal structures was not inevitable — it was engineered, and the ideology justifying it was Marxism.
