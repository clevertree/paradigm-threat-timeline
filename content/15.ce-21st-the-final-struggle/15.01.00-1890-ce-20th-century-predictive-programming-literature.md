# 20th Century Predictive Programming: Literature as Historical Erasure

The decades following [Operation Mockingbird](/timeline/evt-operation-mockingbird) saw the industrialisation of a technique far older than the CIA: **reframing real events as fiction**. From the 1890s onward, a constellation of authors — some conscious operatives, some cultural carriers — systematically converted suppressed history into "fantasy" and "science fiction." The effect was not entertainment. It was **erasure**. Once a real event becomes a novel, anyone who later discovers the truth sounds like they are describing a plot summary.

This is the **backward** direction of [predictive programming](/timeline/evt-predictive-programming): not announcing future operations, but burying past ones under copyright-protected fiction.

## H.G. Wells (1866–1946): The Template

Wells set the pattern. His **Crystal Egg** (1897) describes a crystal sphere that provides real-time visual surveillance of Mars — an observer on Earth watches Martians through it, and the Martians watch back through a matching device. Wells wrote: the crystal was *"sent hither from that planet, in order to give the Martians a near view of our affairs."* This is not a metaphor. It is a description of a two-way scrying device identical to Tolkien's later Palantíri and to the remote-viewing technology declassified under the [Stargate programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stargate_Project) in 1995. Tolkien makes the arc explicit: his Palantíri *"long guarded and united the realm of Gondor"* until Sauron perverted them for subversion. Wells shows only the hostile phase — Martian surveillance for invasion. Same technology, same trajectory: **guard a realm → use for evil**.

*The War of the Worlds* (1898) inverted prior Mars fiction — which portrayed Martians as benign — into an invasion narrative with a **heat ray** matching the U.S. military's later [Active Denial System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_Denial_System). Wells described it as an *invisible* beam generated in "a chamber of practically absolute non-conductivity" and projected by a parabolic mirror—a realistic capacitor-driven directed-energy weapon. Most WotW adaptations changed it to a visible sci-fi laser and dropped the technical specifics. The 2017 authorised sequel (*The Massacre of Mankind*) is one of only two versions (with Jeff Wayne's 1978 album) to keep it invisible—published one year after Havana syndrome (2016) was attributed to directed-energy weapons. See `wget/wells/massacre-of-mankind-2017-reference.md` for the full investigation. Wells's **red weed** appeared six years before the [American chestnut blight](/timeline/evt-american-chestnut-blight) killed four billion trees. His *War in the Air* (1908) described aerial bombing of cities a decade before it happened. His *The World Set Free* (1914) coined the term "atomic bomb" thirty years before Hiroshima.

Wells was not guessing. He was a Fabian Society insider with intelligence connections, and [Florence Deeks demonstrated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florence_Deeks) he plagiarised her unpublished manuscript for *The Outline of History*. His function was to publish real knowledge — past events and planned operations — as fiction so that the general public would treat the information as already-known entertainment rather than disclosure.

### The Plattner Story (1896) and Davidson's Eyes (1895)

*The Plattner Story* sends a chemistry teacher into a **fourth spatial dimension** through a chemical explosion, then returns him with his body **mirror-reversed** (heart on the right side) — physical proof of dimensional displacement. *Davidson's Eyes* describes spontaneous remote viewing: a man in London sees in real-time through the eyes of a lighthouse keeper on a South Sea island. Both stories normalise interdimensional and remote-viewing phenomena as fantasy a century before the U.S. government admitted researching them.

## Arthur Machen (1863–1947): Brain Surgery and Angels

Machen, a **Golden Dawn** initiate, published *The Great God Pan* in 1894 — a story in which a brain surgeon performs a microscopic alteration to open dimensional perception: *"A trifling rearrangement of certain cells, a microscopical alteration… With a touch I can complete the communication between this world of sense and—"* The patient sees beyond the veil and is permanently driven insane. Compare to Lovecraft's *From Beyond* (1920): an electronic machine activating the pineal gland to perceive entities in overlapping dimensions. Same mechanism, updated technology.

More revealing is Machen's *The Angels of Mons* (1915). He wrote a fictional story ("The Bowmen") about spectral medieval archers protecting British troops at the Battle of Mons, published it as fiction in a newspaper — and the public **believed it was a true account**. Machen spent years trying to clarify he had invented it. The legend persisted and was used as wartime propaganda. This is a **documented case of fiction becoming accepted reality** — the reverse of the usual pattern, but equally instructive. If fiction can become fact in the public mind, fact can become fiction just as easily.

## Algernon Blackwood (1869–1951): Thought as Surveillance

Blackwood's *The Willows* (1907), which Lovecraft called the finest supernatural tale in English literature, describes entities from another dimension that detect humans through their **thoughts**: *"Our thoughts make spirals in their world. We must keep them out of our minds at all costs."* The barrier between dimensions is **geographic** — some places have thinner veils: *"a spot held by the dwellers in some outer space, a sort of peep-hole whence they could spy upon the earth, themselves unseen."*

His *John Silence* stories (1908) feature an **occult intelligence operative** — a physician who detects and counters non-human influence as a professional practice. The stories encode the idea that such knowledge is **real, learnable, and operational** — then file it under fiction.

## H.P. Lovecraft (1890–1937): Cosmic Horror as Disclosure

Lovecraft's 104 stories constitute the most systematic fictional encoding of suppressed knowledge produced in the 20th century. The key devices are not metaphors:

- **The Shining Trapezohedron** (*The Haunter of the Dark*, 1935): a crystalline artifact enabling two-way communication with non-human intelligences — a scrying crystal identical in function to Wells's Crystal Egg and Tolkien's Palantíri
- **The resonator** (*From Beyond*, 1920): an electronic device activating the pineal gland to perceive normally-invisible entities occupying the same physical space — functionally identical to Machen's brain surgery in *The Great God Pan*
- **The Great Race of Yith** (*The Shadow out of Time*, 1934–36): entities that project their consciousness across millions of years, swapping minds with beings in other eras to collect intelligence — a systematic description of remote viewing across time
- **The Mi-Go** (*The Whisperer in Darkness*, 1930): alien beings who extract human brains, preserve them in metal cylinders, and transport them across interstellar space — **consciousness separated from biology and stored in technology**
- **The Call of Cthulhu** (1926): a telepathic entity whose dreams are received simultaneously by sensitives worldwide — mass telepathic broadcast

Lovecraft framed all of this as **cosmic horror** — the implication being that knowing these things drives you insane. This is itself a programming layer: *do not investigate; the truth is madness*. Every protagonist who glimpses reality in a Lovecraft story ends up dead, insane, or wishing he had never looked. The message to the reader is: **curiosity about what lies behind the veil is suicidal**.

## Edgar Rice Burroughs (1875–1950): The Unmatched Source

Burroughs's eleven Barsoom novels (1912–1943) describe Mars with structural detail — polar conspiracies, religious fraud as governance, telepathic technology, Kaldane/Rykor symbiosis, brain-transplant (Ras Thavas) — that Percival Lowell's canal theory could not have supplied. Burroughs had **no known intelligence connections**; he was a failed businessman who turned to fiction at 36. The possibility: **access to recording/playback technology or suppressed sources**, pitched as fiction. Same mechanism as Wells's Crystal Egg, Tolkien's Palantíri, Lovecraft's Shining Trapezohedron — and Joseph Smith's seer stones: a device or state yielding indexed information the user could not reach otherwise. Jackson's *Return of the King* omits the Palantír as driver of Gondor's collapse (in the book, Denethor breaks via the seeing-stone); the omission was deliberate. If Burroughs had such access, Barsoom becomes **transcription**, not invention. See [Mars Literature Index](https://paradigm-threat.net/timeline?view=browser&path=investigations/mars/index-mars.md) and [Burroughs investigation](https://paradigm-threat.net/timeline?view=browser&path=investigations/mars/burroughs-barsoom/).

### The Tolkien Convergence

The same intuition applies to J.R.R. Tolkien. Middle-earth's linguistic depth, genealogical consistency, and mythological coherence exceed what any single author could plausibly invent from whole cloth — even an Oxford philologist with access to the source texts. Both men shared a critical condition: **economic need**. Burroughs was broke. Tolkien was an underpaid academic with a large family. Both found that the market paid handsomely for "fiction." Both produced bodies of work whose internal consistency suggests access to pre-existing structured data rather than pure invention. Both are protected by the assumption that they were geniuses. The simpler explanation — that they had access to material they could not acknowledge — is never considered because it would collapse the boundary between fiction and history that the entire genre system depends on.

## J.R.R. Tolkien (1892–1973): The Most Protected Operation

Tolkien presents himself as a translator, not an author. *The Lord of the Rings* is framed as a translation from the **Red Book of Westmarch** — a conceit Tolkien maintained throughout his life and which his son Christopher perpetuated across decades of posthumous publication. The academic term for this is **pseudotranslation**: presenting original work as translation of a lost source.

But Tolkien insisted the frame was real. He described his creative process as **recovery**, not invention: discovering pre-existing truths rather than fabricating stories. Scholar Andrew Higgins wrote that Tolkien *"felt he was recording a past that was already there"* (2015). He wrote that *"the stories were made for the languages"* — that the Elvish languages came first and demanded a mythology to house them. His Elvish (Quenya and Sindarin) demonstrates systematic, internally consistent historical phonology across thousands of years of simulated linguistic evolution — a depth of construction that exceeds any literary conceit.

### The Red Book: Real Manuscript, Fictional Copy

The fictional Red Book of Westmarch is modeled on a **real manuscript**: the **Red Book of Hergest** (*Llyfr Coch Hergest*), Oxford Jesus College MS 111, written c. 1382–1410. Both are red leather-bound compilations containing history, mythology, poetry, and genealogies of a "lost civilisation." Tolkien, who spent his career at Oxford, had direct access to it. The real Red Book contains the **Mabinogion** — the foundational Welsh mythology — and was translated by Lady Charlotte Guest in the 1840s as, effectively, **"a mythology for Wales."** Tolkien scholar Mark T. Hooker (2006) confirms the naming is deliberate.

Tolkien went further than textual framing. He **physically forged artifacts** — using his pipe to burn edge-marks onto pages of the Book of Mazarbul, piercing stitch-holes to simulate parchment binding, and washing them with red paint to resemble bloodstains (Catherine McIlwaine, *Tolkien: Maker of Middle-earth*, 2018). Scholar Nick Groom places him in the tradition of Thomas Chatterton and the literary forgers — men who fabricated "ancient manuscripts" modeled on things they *believed* had existed.

### The Silmarillion Is Not Original

Cross-referencing *The Silmarillion* against the source texts Tolkien drew from reveals not "inspiration" but **systematic reassembly** of existing myths under new names: Ainulindalë → Kalevala creation; Silmarils → Sampo; Túrin → Kullervo; Beren's hand → Týr and Fenrir; Gondolin → Troy + Ragnarök; Akallabêth → Atlantis + Flood; One Ring → Andvari's Ring. ### The Dvergatal: Verbatim Name Theft

The most undeniable evidence. The Völuspá (Elder Edda, stanzas 10–16) contains the **Dvergatal** — the "Catalogue of Dwarves." Tolkien took the names of virtually *all* his Dwarf characters, plus Gandalf, **directly from this list**:

> *"Then was Môtsognir created greatest of all the dwarfs, and **Durin** second… **Dvalin** Nâr and Nâin, Niping, Dain, Bivör, Bavör, **Bömbur**, **Nori**… Veig and **Gandâlf**, Vindâlf, **Thrain**, Thekk and **Thorin**, **Thrôr**… **Fili**, **Kili**, Fundin, Nali… **Eikinskialdi**."*

Thorin, Thrór, Thráin, Durin, Dwalin, Fíli, Kíli, Nori, Bombur, Glóin, Dáin, Ori, Fundin — all **verbatim from the Edda**. "Eikinskialdi" translates directly as **Oakenshield** (eik = oak, skialdi = shield). Even "Gandalf" is a *dwarf name* in the original — Tolkien repurposed it for his wizard. The entire company of Thorin Oakenshield in *The Hobbit* was lifted from a single page of the Elder Edda.

### Snorri's Euhemerism: The Interpretive Key

Tolkien's primary Norse source, Snorri Sturluson's Prose Edda, explicitly states in its Prologue that **the Norse gods were real historical rulers from Asia Minor**:

> *"Near the middle of the world was built the house and inn… which was called **Troy**, in the land which we call Turkey… all rulers of the north region **trace their ancestors back thither**, and place in the number of the gods all who were rulers of the city. Especially do they place **Priamos himself in the stead of Odin**."*

> *"**Odin fled out of Asia** and hither to the north country, and then he gave to himself and his men their names… the tongue of those **Asiamen became the native tongue of all these lands**."*

Snorri's position is unambiguous: mythology is distorted history. The gods were real kings who migrated from Troy to Scandinavia and were later deified. Tolkien, as a Norse philologist who spent his career studying Snorri's works, worked within a tradition that **explicitly stated myths are distorted chronicles of real events.**

### The Time-Travel Novels: Myths Are Real

Tolkien wrote two unfinished novels — *The Lost Road* (1937) and *The Notion Club Papers* (1944–46) — about **Oxford academics who discover that ancient myths are literally true** through ancestral memory and dream-visions. In both, a modern character experiences vivid recall of Númenor's fall. The frame story of the *Notion Club Papers*: a man finds the documents *"in sacks of waste paper at Oxford in 2012."* Tolkien was writing stories about people **exactly like himself** discovering that the myths he studied were real memories. Why would a philologist write this unless he at least entertained the possibility?

### The Fomenko Parallel

Fomenko and Nosovskiy's *What Shakespeare Actually Wrote About* (2016) demonstrates that Shakespeare's plays are literary reworkings of real 12th–16th century events — the histories of the Rus-Horde dressed in Italian and English costume. Tolkien performs the same operation: The War of the Ring maps onto actual medieval conflicts; the Palantíri are remote-viewing devices described across multiple independent traditions; the Númenórean catastrophe encodes planetary cataclysm; the Rohirrim are Eurasian steppe horse-lords; Gondor is Byzantium in decline; Minas Tirith is Constantinople. Like Shakespeare, Tolkien's "fiction" is a **literary reworking of suppressed history**. ### The Copyright Shield

**Tolkien's works remain under copyright until 2043** (life + 70 years under EU/UK law; U.S. copyright on individual works extends even further based on publication date — *The Silmarillion* published 1977 remains protected until at least **2072**). This is not incidental. Copyright prevents independent researchers from republishing, annotating, cross-referencing, or computationally analysing the full texts without permission of the Tolkien Estate. The longest-running literary operation in modern history is also the most legally protected.

## Orson Scott Card (b. 1951): The Mormon Channel

Card (LDS, great-great-grandson of Brigham Young) published *Ender's Game* in 1985: a child unknowingly commands planetary destruction via "simulations" that are real battles; telepathic hive-mind enemy; secondary discharge destroys the attacker's fleet; dictator rises via media manipulation; truth told as fiction. The U.S. Marine Corps placed it on their Reading List. Drone warfare replicates the scenario. Card's novel functions as predictive programming, training manual, and encoded history — the [Mars catastrophe](/timeline/evt-mars-catastrophe-brandenburg) retold. The pattern (planet attacks planet, suffers blow-back) appears in Wells, Lovecraft, Lucas, Hubbard, *Stargate* — suggesting a **common source**.

## The Copyright Shield

Copyright law protects these encodings from deconstruction. Tolkien's texts cannot be freely republished, annotated, or computationally cross-referenced until **2043 at the earliest**. Lovecraft is the exception — his works entered the public domain between 2007 and 2032 (depending on jurisdiction), which is why Lovecraft's texts are now publicly available while Tolkien's remain locked. This is not a flaw in the system. **Copyright functions as classification** — keeping the most detailed encodings inaccessible to independent analysis for a century after publication.

Card's works remain under active copyright with no near-term expiration. Wells and Machen are in the public domain. Blackwood entered the public domain in 2022 (UK) and varies by work in the US.
