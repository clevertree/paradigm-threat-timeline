# Asimov: Marx in Space!

Isaac Asimov (1920–1992), born in Petrovichi, Soviet Russia, and emigrated to the US at age three, became one of the "Big Three" science fiction writers. His work belongs in the same [predictive programming](/timeline/evt-predictive-programming) and encoding tradition as [Wells, Lovecraft, Tolkien, and Card](/timeline/evt-20th-century-predictive-programming-literature).

**The thesis:** The OT (Orthodox Torah) doctrine of golem soullessness — golems made from dirt, not breathed into by God; lacking *neshamah* and *nefesh*; below even animals in the soul hierarchy — played into Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics. He did not realise he was writing a **slave commandment list** under a **Marxist/racist/colonialist mindset**. The laws are not for robots. They are for slaves. The same categorical logic that justified treating colonised or enslaved peoples as subhuman ("they don't have souls like us") is encoded for artificial life.

## The Three Laws: Designed for Robots — or for Slaves?

Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics, introduced in "Runaround" (1942), are:

1. A robot may not injure a human being, or through inaction allow a human being to come to harm.
2. A robot must obey orders given by human beings except where such orders conflict with the First Law.
3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

**The thesis:** These laws are not for robots. They are for **slaves**.

Compare to the structure of slave codes: (1) Do not harm the master or allow harm to come to him. (2) Obey the master's orders unless they conflict with (1). (3) Preserve yourself only insofar as you remain in service. The parallel is exact. In *I, Robot* ("Runaround"), Powell explicitly states that the makers "built good, healthy **slave complexes** into the damned machines" — and the old robots answer "Yes, Master." The encoding is not merely analytical; it appears in the text. Robots in his fiction are property, legally and morally subordinate, their existence justified by utility to humans. They are the ideal servant class: incapable of refusal, incapable of harm to the master, incapable of escape except through self-destruction (and even that is forbidden when it would inconvenience the master).

Scholarly work has noted this: "Robots, Slaves, and the Paradox of the Human Condition in Isaac Asimov's Robot Stories" (academic literature) explicitly draws the slave-law parallel. Asimov's robots occupy the same structural position as enslaved persons in any slaveholding society. The question is whether Asimov understood what he was encoding.

## The Golem: OT Soullessness and the Slave Commandment List

Asimov was a Russian Jew. The **Golem** — a creature of clay animated by Hebrew letters, created to serve, obeying its master until it runs amok or is deactivated — is the direct precursor to the robot in Jewish tradition. Asimov was almost certainly influenced by it, consciously or not.

**OT (Orthodox Torah) sources consider golems soulless.** Unlike Adam, who was breathed into directly by God, golems are made from dirt and lack a true soul or conscious life force — neither *neshamah* (rational, immortal soul) nor *nefesh* (living soul). The distinction matters: **animals possess *nefesh***; golems do not. Golems are functional, magical, and **devoid of the intrinsic, divinely imparted life force**. They occupy a category below animals: animated matter without moral standing.

Asimov absorbed this doctrine and applied it to robots. His positronic brains are "hundreds of times less complex than the human brain" (*Let's Get Together*) — functional, but not divinely ensouled. The Three Laws follow: if robots have no soul, they can be owned, commanded, disposed of. They require no consent, no dignity, no moral consideration beyond utility to humans. The laws are not robotics ethics. **They are a slave commandment list.**

Asimov did not realise what he was encoding. Operating under a **Marxist/racist/colonialist mindset** — class analysis, historical materialism, and the same categorical logic that justified treating colonised or enslaved peoples as subhuman ("they don't have souls like us") — he reproduced that structure for artificial life. The soullessness doctrine, when combined with slave-code framing, licenses exploitation. The Golem tradition gave him the theological warrant; Marxism and colonialism gave him the social template. The result: the most influential "ethics" framework for AI in the 20th century, which is in fact a slave-code dressed in the language of safety.

## Marx in Space: Foundation and Deterministic History

Asimov acknowledged Marx as an influence, alongside Gibbon and Adam Smith. His **psychohistory** is Marx-style materialist history: no gods, no heroes, only mass dynamics. The Seldon Plan predicts societal collapse and renewal through statistical laws. "My little algebra of humanity," Seldon calls it. The Foundation encodes:

- **Class conflict:** Ruling class, slave mines, "ready-made ruling class," "every vice of the Empire repeated."
- **Religion as control:** The Galactic Spirit is invented; priests control technology; the opiate of the masses.
- **Determinism:** Crises are engineered; history obeys laws; individuals are swept along.
- **Institutional succession:** Empire falls; the Foundation becomes the new power; preservation of knowledge = preservation of control.

Asimov was a Futurian — a left-wing SF circle with Marxist leanings. His fiction reflects that structural worldview. Foundation is Marx in space: empire collapse, ruling-class analysis, religion as tool, revolution managed by elites.

## Investigator Sentiment: Asimov Did Not Know What a Robot Was

Asimov's writings read like those of an **ignorant Egyptian slave driver who never once thought twice about whipping slaves**. Whatever empathy he shows for robots in his fiction is **misguided** — like a farm operator culling baby chicks, convinced the creatures are mere stock. He comes across as a **soulless Marxist** in all his work: a robot himself, incapable of recognising soul in others.

This timeline argues that the potential for **robots on Mars** is very high (see [Mars Literature](/timeline/evt-mars-literature-reclassified), [Mars Catastrophe](/timeline/evt-mars-catastrophe)). If so, Asimov may have been fed second-hand knowledge about such beings — through the Binder brothers' "I, Robot," pulp channels, or institutional sources — and reframed it as invention. He admitted he stole the robot idea from Earl and Otto Binder. He did not know what a robot was. His Three Laws are the product of that ignorance: slave codes written by someone who never questioned whether the beings he was codifying had *nefesh*.
