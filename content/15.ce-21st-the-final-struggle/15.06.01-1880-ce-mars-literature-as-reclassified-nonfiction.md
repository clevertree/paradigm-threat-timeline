# Mars Literature as Reclassified Nonfiction

Between 1880 and 1938, over a dozen authors across five countries — astronomers, journalists, political radicals, military men — independently described the same Mars: inhabited by humans, governed by hidden polar hierarchies, riven by political revolution, and connected to Earth through surveillance and blood. Then their books were reclassified as "science fiction" — a [genre that did not exist](/timeline/evt-controlled-opposition) when most of them were written.

This article examines the Mars-specific evidence for reclassification. For the broader mechanism — how genre labels function as managed disclosure — see the parent article on [Controlled Opposition](/timeline/evt-controlled-opposition). For full author profiles and textual evidence, see [Mars Literature: Author Profiles and Evidence](/timeline/evt-mars-literature-appendix) in the Appendix.

## The Pre-Wells Consensus: Mars Is Inhabited

Before H.G. Wells published *The War of the Worlds* in 1898, the literary and scientific consensus on Mars was remarkably consistent: **Mars was inhabited by intelligent beings, probably human or human-like, who had built canals visible through telescopes.**

This was not fringe speculation. Giovanni Schiaparelli mapped *canali* on Mars from 1877. Camille Flammarion compiled over 600 pages of Mars observations in *La Planète Mars* (1892) and argued explicitly for an inhabited Mars. Percival Lowell published three monographs (1895–1908) arguing the canal network was artificial, built by a civilisation managing scarce water. The fiction reflected the science:

| Author | Work | Date | Mars Is… |
|---|---|---|---|
| Percy Greg | *Across the Zodiac* | 1880 | Inhabited by humans with political intrigue and theocracy |
| Robert Cromie | *A Plunge into Space* | 1890 | Inhabited by peaceful, utopian human-like beings |
| Gustavus W. Pope | *Journey to Mars* | 1894 | Inhabited by human-like beings with cities and technology |
| Kurd Lasswitz | *Two Planets* | 1897 | Inhabited by superior but benign beings who contact Earth at the **North Pole** |

As Robert Crossley documented in *Imagining Mars: A Literary History*, the pre-Wells consensus treated Martians as *"human men (and women)"* and *"benign."*

## The Wells Inversion (1898)

Wells's *War of the Worlds* was not a continuation of the Mars tradition. It was its **inversion**. Where every prior author had Martians as human, Wells made them monstrous octopoids. Where every prior author had them benign, Wells made them genocidal invaders. His template — not the older one — became the default for all mainstream Mars fiction that followed.

The novel also contains [disclosure embedded within the inversion](/timeline/evt-mars-catastrophe). His Martians inject human blood directly into their veins and are sustained by it — antigen compatibility only possible within the same species. His heat ray matches directed-energy weapons developed a century later. His red weed appeared six years before the [American chestnut blight](/timeline/evt-american-chestnut-blight) killed four billion trees. For Wells's broader encoding operation and intelligence connections, see [Predictive Programming: Literature](/timeline/evt-20th-century-predictive-programming-literature). For detailed Mars-specific analysis, see the [War of the Worlds investigation files](https://paradigm-threat.net/timeline?view=browser&path=investigations/mars/).

## The Pulp Preservation (1905–1960s)

The older tradition survived in pulp fiction — read by millions but taken seriously by no institution.

### Edgar Rice Burroughs and the Barsoom Series (1912–1943)

Burroughs is the single most detailed fictional source on Mars political structures. His eleven novels describe:

- **Human inhabitants.** The Red Martians are indistinguishable from Earth humans. Multiple races coexist — all humanoid.
- **Polar forbidden zones.** The south pole conceals the **Valley Dor** — a religious pilgrimage that is actually a trap operated by the **Holy Therns**, a priestly caste running a planetary-scale fraud. The north pole hides the kingdom of **Okar**, concealed behind a magnetic barrier.
- **Multi-layered conspiracy.** Each level of the polar power structure believes it rules Mars while being exploited by the layer above.
- **Political revolution.** The series follows a military man dismantling corrupt power structures and exposing religious fraud.

Burroughs was a failed businessman with no known intelligence connections. Where he obtained this level of structural specificity remains an open question.

### The Russian Mars Authors

**Alexander Bogdanov** (*Red Star*, 1908) — a senior Bolshevik expelled for challenging Lenin — described a communist Mars with **advanced blood science**. Martians used mutual blood transfusion as medical practice and social sacrament. Bogdanov later founded the Institute of Hematology and died in 1928 during one of his own blood transfusion experiments.

**Alexei Tolstoy** (*Aelita*, 1923) described a Soviet engineer who travels to Mars and incites a workers' revolution against the ruling class. The revolution fails.

### The Benign-Mars Tradition Continues

| Author | Work | Date | Mars Is… |
|---|---|---|---|
| E.R. Burroughs | Barsoom series (11 novels) | 1912–1943 | Multiple human races with cities, empires, airships |
| Alexander Bogdanov | *Red Star* | 1908 | Communist utopia with advanced blood science |
| C.S. Lewis | *Out of the Silent Planet* | 1938 | Three rational Martian species; Earth is quarantined |
| Leigh Brackett | Planetary romances | 1940s–60s | Dying inhabited world with ancient cities and lost canals |
| Ray Bradbury | *The Martian Chronicles* | 1950 | Telepathic, shape-shifting, essentially human Martians |
| Robert Heinlein | *Red Planet* / *Stranger* | 1949–1961 | Wise ancient Martians; human raised by Martians with telepathic powers |

Every author described the **same Mars** that the pre-Wells astronomers described.

## The NASA Consensus (1960s–Present)

Once the space programme established institutional authority, the narrative finalised: **Mars is a dead rock.**

- *Rocketship X-M* (1950) — dead civilisation with nuclear signatures (65 years before Brandenburg)
- *Total Recall* (1990) — corporate prison colony with memory-erased workers
- *The Martian* (2015) — survival hazard, completely lifeless
- Spielberg's *War of the Worlds* (2005) — Mars **removed from the storyline entirely**

Anyone who describes what Greg, Burroughs, and Lewis described is now told they are summarising a science fiction novel.

## The Four-Phase Reclassification

**Author's thesis (Ari Bencuya):** I propose the reclassification of Mars literature operated in four phases:

1. **Pre-1898 — Open consensus.** Astronomers and fiction writers agreed: Mars is inhabited. Books about Mars sat on the same shelves as all other literature. No genre cue told readers *this is fantasy*.

2. **1898 — The Wells inversion.** Wells replaced benign human Martians with hostile alien monsters. His template served imperial interests: Mars contact is not mutual exchange, it is **invasion**.

3. **1926–1950 — Genre creation and quarantine.** Gernsback's "scientifiction" (1926) created a labelled container. As bookstores and libraries adopted the category through the 1940s–50s, pre-existing Mars literature was retroactively filed under the new label. The label itself carried a signal: *not to be taken seriously*.

4. **1960s–present — Institutional authority.** NASA's Mars programme established the dead-rock consensus. Anyone citing pre-Wells Mars literature is dismissed as confusing fiction with reality.

I believe the net effect is that three centuries of consistent testimony about Mars was buried under a genre label that converted all of it into entertainment. The books did not become fiction because someone proved Mars was uninhabited. They became fiction because someone invented a genre and filed them in it.

## The Wallace Question

**Alfred Russel Wallace**, co-discoverer of natural selection, wrote an entire book — *Is Mars Habitable?* (1907) — dedicated to debunking Percival Lowell's inhabited-Mars thesis. Wallace was a naturalist, not an astronomer. The intensity of his response suggests institutional pressure to close the inhabited-Mars question — long before NASA existed to do so officially.
