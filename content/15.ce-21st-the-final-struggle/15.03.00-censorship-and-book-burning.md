# Censorship and Book Burning: From Alexandria to the Internet

## The Constant

There is one activity that every regime, every conquering army, and every new orthodoxy has performed without exception: **the destruction of the previous era's records**. The method changes — fire, edict, index, algorithm — but the function never varies. Whoever controls the past controls the present.

This article traces the pattern of deliberate record destruction from antiquity to the digital age, with emphasis on the understudied 20th century campaigns that shaped the historical record we inherit today.

## The Historical Record

The pattern of deliberate record destruction stretches from antiquity to the present. Key episodes include:

- **The Library of Alexandria** — repeatedly targeted across centuries by multiple factions (Caesar, Theophilus, Caliph Omar); under Fomenko's analysis, these may represent a single event duplicated across phantom centuries
- **Qin Shi Huang** (213 BC, Scaligerian) — burned all books except agriculture, medicine, and divination; buried scholars alive
- **Roman Imperial censorship** — destruction of Etruscan, Druidic, and Sibylline records
- **Christianisation campaigns** — monastic "preservation" meant selective copying; Bishop de Landa burned all but four Mayan codices (1562); the Index Librorum Prohibitorum ran from 1559 to 1966
- **Scaliger and Petavius** (16th–17th c.) — constructed the fraudulent chronological framework, enforced through institutional authority; records that contradicted it were reinterpreted rather than destroyed
- **Bolshevik Revolution** (1917) — destroyed Tsarist archives, eliminated the intellectual class, rewrote encyclopedias in real time
- **Nazi book burning** (1933) — 25,000 books burned in Berlin alone; the regime secretly preserved seized material for its own use
- **Allied bombing** (1943–45) — destroyed vast German historical archives stretching back centuries
- **Operation Paperclip** (1945) — American and Soviet seizure of German records; much remains classified
- **Chinese Cultural Revolution** (1966–76) — systematic destruction of temples, classical texts, genealogical records under the "Four Olds" campaign
- **Operation Mockingbird** (1948–75+) — shifted from destroying records to controlling the narrative surrounding them

For the detailed account of each episode, see [Censorship and Book Burning: The Historical Record](/timeline/evt-censorship-historical-timeline) in the Appendix.

## 21st Century: Digital Censorship

The internet initially appeared to democratise access to information. That window is closing:

### Platform Monopoly and De-platforming

The concentration of communication into a handful of corporate platforms (Discord, YouTube, Facebook/Meta, Google) created new chokepoints for censorship:

- **YouTube's algorithm changes** (2019–present) systematically suppress independent research content while promoting institutional sources
- **Discord's moderation policies** enable permanent bans that sever researchers from their communities with no appeal process and no requirement to specify the violation
- **Google's search algorithm** increasingly buries independent research sites in favour of institutional sources

The author's own permanent ban from Discord — the primary communication platform for independent chronology and cosmology researchers — illustrates the pattern. No specific violation was cited. The effect was immediate severance from collaborative research networks built over years.

### Digital Memory Holes

Websites disappear. Archives are de-indexed. Search results are filtered. The internet's apparent permanence masks a reality of continuous, silent erasure:

- **Wayback Machine limitations** — sites can request removal from the Internet Archive
- **Link rot** — studies estimate 25% of web pages from 2013 are no longer accessible
- **Algorithmic suppression** — content need not be deleted to be made invisible; it need only be de-ranked

### The Pattern Continues

The method has changed from fire to algorithm, but the function is identical: **control which records survive, control which narrative prevails**. The Mayan codex burner and the platform moderator serve the same master — not necessarily consciously, but structurally.

## The Investigator's Response

Given this millennia-long pattern, the investigator must assume:

1. **Every surviving record has been filtered.** What we have is what the victors permitted us to have.
2. **Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.** The gap in the record may be the most important datum.
3. **Redundancy is survival.** Information preserved in multiple traditions, across multiple continents, is harder to erase. This is why mythological analysis — comparing traditions from Mesoamerica, Mesopotamia, Egypt, India, and China — is essential.
4. **The internet is not permanent.** Critical sources should be archived locally. The wget archive that supports this timeline project exists precisely because websites vanish.
