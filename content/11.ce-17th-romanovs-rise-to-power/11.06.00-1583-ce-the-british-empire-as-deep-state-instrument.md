## The Thesis

The British Empire (1583–1997) was not a conventional empire. Unlike Rome, the Mongols, the Horde, or the Ottomans, the British Empire was **not interested in retaining its colonies**. It conquered territory, divided the local population along racial or ethnic markers, armed both sides with advanced weapons, established financial control — and then **left**, deliberately, at the moment calculated to produce maximum chaos.

This strategy did not benefit Britain and could only come from a hypothetical Pyramidal Empire, issued in the same form across every region.

## The Method

1. **Arrive** — establish military and commercial presence via missionary activity or "civilizing" claims
2. **Map and divide** — intensive ethnographic surveys identifying every racial, tribal, religious, and linguistic fault line
3. **Arm both sides** — supply modern weapons to competing factions
4. **Establish financial control** — centralized currency, debt dependencies, extraction infrastructure
5. **Leave at the right moment** — withdraw when armed, divided populations will turn on each other
6. **"Order from chaos"** — maintain invisible control through aristocratic and financial ties over successor states too divided and indebted to resist

## Case Studies

**India and Pakistan (1947):** Two centuries of deepening the Hindu-Muslim divide produced the [Partition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_of_India) — 15 million displaced, up to 2 million dead. The resulting states have fought four wars and remain in nuclear standoff dependent on Western arms and finance.

**Israel (1917–1948):** The [Balfour Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balfour_Declaration) promised a Jewish homeland while Britain simultaneously encouraged Jewish immigration and armed Arab resistance. The 1948 withdrawal produced immediate war. Both sides remain dependent on Western military and financial support.

**Africa (1880s–1960s):** The [Berlin Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa) (1884) drew borders deliberately cutting across ethnic boundaries. Colonial administration hardened tribal divisions (Hutu-Tutsi in Rwanda). Independence in the 1960s produced civil wars — Nigeria/Biafra, Sudan, Rwanda, Congo — killing millions.

**Japan and China:** British interests broke Chinese sovereignty via the [Opium Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_Wars) while British engagement with Japan during the [Meiji Restoration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiji_Restoration) created a modern military power. The resulting Sino-Japanese rivalry served Western interests by keeping Asia's largest powers focused on each other.

**Hong Kong (1997):** Decades of constructing institutions incompatible with the mainland system — independent judiciary, separate currency, press culture — produced a permanent pressure point for Western geopolitical leverage.
