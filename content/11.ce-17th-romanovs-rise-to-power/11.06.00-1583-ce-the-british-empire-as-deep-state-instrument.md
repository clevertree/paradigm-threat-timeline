## The Thesis

The British Empire (1583–1997) was not a conventional empire. Unlike Rome, the Mongols, the Horde, or the Ottomans, the British Empire **did not want to retain its colonies**. It conquered territory, divided populations along racial/ethnic markers, armed both sides with advanced weapons, established financial control — then **left** at the moment calculated to produce maximum chaos.

This strategy did not benefit Britain and could only come from a hypothetical Pyramidal Empire.

2. **Map and divide** — ethnographic surveys identifying every fault line
3. **Arm both sides** — supply modern weapons to competing factions
4. **Establish financial control** — centralized currency, debt dependencies

## Case Studies

**India and Pakistan (1947):** Two centuries deepening the Hindu-Muslim divide produced [Partition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_of_India) — 15 million displaced, up to 2 million dead. Resulting states fought four wars and remain in nuclear standoff dependent on Western arms and finance.

**Israel (1917–1948):** The [Balfour Declaration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balfour_Declaration) promised a Jewish homeland while Britain encouraged Jewish immigration *and* armed Arab resistance. The 1948 withdrawal produced immediate war. Both sides remain Western-dependent.

**Africa (1880s–1960s):** The [Berlin Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa) (1884) drew borders cutting across ethnic boundaries. Colonial administration hardened tribal divisions (Hutu-Tutsi in Rwanda). 1960s independence produced civil wars killing millions.

**Japan and China:** British interests broke Chinese sovereignty via [Opium Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_Wars) while engagement during [Meiji Restoration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiji_Restoration) created Japanese military power. The Sino-Japanese rivalry keeps Asia's largest powers focused on each other.

**Hong Kong (1997):** Decades constructing incompatible institutions — independent judiciary, separate currency — produced a permanent pressure point for Western leverage.
