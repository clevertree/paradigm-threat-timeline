## London's Rebuilding and New Financial Order

Political aftermath is as significant as fire itself. When rebuilding plan was implemented, medieval guildhall structure and warren of organic property rights defining old City was replaced with regularized street grid — conveniently amenable to new financial architecture being planned. The Cestui Que Vie Act of 1666, passed same year as fire, created legal presumption that any person absent from estate for seven years could be declared legally dead, with estate administered by others. Long-term implications for property law, inheritance, and formation of financial trusts governing Western institutional life have never been fully traced.

## NC Context: England as New Financial Metropoly

In Fomenko's reconstruction ([Ch.7 §18](https://chronologia.org/en/how_it_was/07_15.html)), England's rise to global financial dominance was not organic — it was funded by stolen treasury of Hordian Empire, moved to British Isles specifically because geographically remote from Russia-Horde and any potential imperial restoration. Fire of London served this project by destroying physical evidence of old order (87 churches, medieval Guildhall, St. Paul's Cathedral, thousands of private records) and forcing complete legal and financial reset. Royal Exchange, one of few institutions that might have anchored independent financial system, was among structures destroyed.

That Charles II — himself restored only six years earlier — attempted to insert royal troops into City during crisis and was blocked by City Magistrates illustrates how thoroughly London's financial elite had consolidated their independence from Crown. Within twenty-eight years, Bank of England would formalize that independence into institutional law.
