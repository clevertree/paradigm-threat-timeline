# Gregorian Calendar makes slight

In 1582, Pope Gregory XIII introduced the Gregorian calendar, ostensibly to correct a 10-day drift in the Julian calendar since the Council of Nicaea (325 CE). The reform deleted 10 days from October 1582 and adjusted the leap year rule — changing the average year from 365.25 to 365.2425 days. This created the Old Style/New Style split persisting today — Russia did not adopt the Gregorian calendar until 1918.

## The Stated Reason vs. the True Reason

The official justification: the vernal equinox had drifted from March 21 to March 11, threatening the Paschal calendar's accuracy for computing Easter.

Fomenko and Nosovskiy demonstrate in [Ch.7 §34](https://chronologia.org/en/how_it_was/07_34.html) that the Paschal calendar (Computus) **could not have been established at Nicaea in 325 CE**:

> The Council which established Paschal could not have taken place earlier than 784, as only beginning with this year, due to the slow astronomical shift of the moon phases, the concurrencies of the calendar Christian Easter and the Jewish Passover had ceased.

The fundamental rule — Easter must **not** coincide with Jewish Passover — would have been violated repeatedly in the IV century. The Computus was actually compiled no earlier than the VIII–XI centuries. The Gregorian reform corrected a much shorter drift while **creating the false impression** of continuous Church calendar use since the IV century.

## The Scaligerian Year Zero

The reform was connected to Scaligerian chronology construction. The Anno Domini system was supposedly calculated by Dionysius Exiguus in the VI century. Fomenko notes:

> *"Since the beginning of A.D. there was no continuous yearly calculation until the current year. The first year 'A.D.' was calculated much later as the year of the Nativity of Christ."*

Systematic Anno Domini use began only from 1431 — over 900 years after supposedly devised. Fomenko proposes "Dionysius the Small" (*Exiguus*) is a phantom of XVII century chronologist **Dionysius Petavius** (French *petit* = "small" → *Petavius*).

The Gregorian reform (1582), the Anno Domini era, and Scaliger's *De emendatione temporum* (1583) were **simultaneous, coordinated operations** replacing the Empire's existing calendar systems — including the Byzantine era (creation at 5508 BC) — with a framework pushing Christ ~1,053 years into the past and creating the illusion of unbroken Church history since antiquity.
