# Giants are defeated at the Battle of Kulikovo

The [Battle of Kulikovo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Kulikovo) (~1380 CE) was the decisive turning-point of recorded history. The [giants of the Horde](/content/07.ce-13th-the-russian-horde-tartarian-empire/07.01.02-the-giants-of-the-rus-horde.md) were defeated by the new "Marathon" cannon technology of smaller humans. **Royal (Hereditary) Christianity** fell; **Apostles' Christianity** triumphed. The giants lost authority over the Rus-Horde Empire and became permanent enemies of the state: enslaved, hunted, eradicated.

*The Battle of Kulikovo marks the moment cannon technology overcame armored giants — remembered as David vs Goliath and the Battle of Marathon.*

## The Weapon That Changed Everything

Metal cannons — deployed en masse for the first time — could penetrate the heavy armour that had made giants invincible. The theological significance was immediate: **Dmitry Donskoi = Constantine the Great** baptised the Empire into Apostles' Christianity and established hereditary czarist succession — replacing the older system where giant clan-leaders rotated power.

## Thirty Phantom Reflections

Fomenko identifies Kulikovo as the most duplicated event in history, reflected across **at least 30 phantom copies**:

- **David and Goliath** (1 Samuel 17) — A young king defeats a *giant* with a "sling" (= cannon). Both religions enshrined this as their founding myth of courage.
- **The Gallic War giant** — Roman histories record a monstrous champion slain by a smaller soldier; the "barbarian" army collapses immediately.
- **King Arthur and the giant** — Geoffrey of Monmouth has Arthur fight "a fierce giant" at Mont-Saint-Michel, then conquer the continent.
- **Zeus and the Titans** — The gods overthrow the Titans with help from the [Cyclops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclops) — a transparent metaphor for *cannons*: a single "eye" (= muzzle) hurling thunderbolts (= cannonballs).
- **Battle of Marathon** (490 BCE) — "Javelins" defeat a vastly larger army. Greek city-states (smaller humans) prevail against the Persian Empire (the Horde). Chronological distance (~1870 years) is a classic Fomenko shift.
- **Constantine defeats Maxentius** — Fomenko's primary identification: Donskoi *is* Constantine I; the Roman Empire's conversion to Christianity *is* the Horde's conversion after Kulikovo.

Each narrative preserves a common skeleton: a smaller champion overcomes giants with a new weapon or divine intervention. The sheer number of duplicates testifies to the event's traumatic importance.
