# The 225-Day Year

*Evidence from the Sumerian King-List*

Saturn was called [Kronos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cronus), the *God before time began*. Before clocks and calendars, planetary positions were the only way to measure time. When Saturn's system joined the Sun's orbit, time began. Our current Sun was still hidden behind Saturn (and Jupiter). Earth's spin remained constant — a "day" stayed the same — but the year was only 225 days: 40% faster than today's 365.25-day orbit. Year-lengths shifted periodically as Earth jumped between orbits, each jump drawing less solar energy and producing a slower orbit.

## Kronos = Time

Saturn or Kronos translates to "time." The visible crescent of Saturn — lit by the real Sun at Saturn's rim — was the first clock. As Earth rotated beneath Saturn, this crescent swept counterclockwise, brightening at night and dimming by day.

> *"We can actually claim that Saturn invented time — when it no longer blazed, but had reduced its output or dropped to dark mode. That is when a crescent would be seen at the edge of the globe, rotating counterclockwise on a daily basis."*
> — Jno Cook, *Saturnian Cosmology*, Chapter 14

When Jupiter displaced Saturn after 3147 BC, Jupiter replaced "the older God of Time and Beginnings."

Sumerian King-List (*List of Kings Before the Flood*) — reigns in tens of thousands of "years" dismissed as fantastical. Cook: these record *days*; only a 225-day year resolves totals into whole-number multiples of 32 years. Grand total: 241,200 days = 1,072 years. A 225-day year = 0.72 AU (Venus's orbit); Kepler's third law gives ~214 days for 0.70 AU — consistent. The 360→365.24 jump (747 BC) corroborated by calendar reforms across Egypt, Rome, Greece, Mesopotamia. C-14: shorter-year chronology tracks INTCAL98 calibration.
