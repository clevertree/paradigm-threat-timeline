# The Rebellion of Pugachev

In 1774 Pugachev's rebellion peaked. Claiming to be Peter III and direct descendant of Christ, Pugachev was positioned to reclaim the entire Tartarian Empire and overthrow the foreign Romanovs. Had he succeeded, Russia could have returned to former glory, re-imposing a Second Inquisition that would have erased the Reformation, state monotheism, slavery, and usury.

Pugachev was a giant human, as were much of his army. He was Peter III — actual heir to the Russian throne, not an impostor as Romanov historians claim. He led both giants and smaller humans against Romanov authority. This was the third major war between humans and giants. The first was the 12th-century Crusade. The second was the [Battle of Kulikovo](/timeline/evt-giants-are-defeated-at-the-battle-of-kulikovo) (1380), where cannon technology defeated the giants. By Pugachev's time, giants had their own cannons and had mastered guerrilla warfare. His rebellion threatened every military-religious order worldwide.

## Moscow Tartary: The War's True Stakes

At the time (1773–1775), Moscow Tartary still existed as a functional sovereign state — labeled the largest country in the world in the 1771 *Encyclopaedia Britannica*. Its capital was Tobolsk (Biblical Tubal); its territory spanned from the Volga across the Urals, Siberia, Alaska, and North America west of the Atlantic settlements.

The "rebellion" was not a peasant uprising — it was war between two Russian states. The Tobolsk czar launched an offensive against Romanov Russia from the east, simultaneously with the Ottoman Empire attacking from the south. Fomenko writes:

> *"The conflict between Moscow Tartary and Romanov Russia ended in the second half of the XVIII century with the famous, allegedly 'peasant', war against 'Pugachev'. The Romanovs succeeded in agreeing peace separately with Turkey and defeating the Great Tartary."*
> — Fomenko, [Ch.8 §1](https://chronologia.org/en/how_it_was/08.html)

## Suvorov Defeated Pugachev — Concealed

Official Russian history credits victory to Lieutenant-Colonel Mikhailson. Fomenko demonstrates it was **Alexander Suvorov** — the century's greatest Russian commander — who personally led the decisive campaign. Concealment was deliberate: the Romanovs needed to present the conflict as a minor disturbance, not a massive campaign requiring their finest general.

Suvorov's title "Count of Rymnik" (supposedly for an 1789 battle in Romania) traces to the real battle on the Yaik/Rymnik River against Pugachev in 1774. A Romanian river was later renamed "Rymnik" to provide cover:

> *"It turns out that A.V.Suvorov's distinguished title of 'Count of Rymnik' is not related to the geographical name of 'Rymnik' in Romania… but to the old name of the Yaik River…"*
> — Fomenko, [Ch.8 §3](https://chronologia.org/en/how_it_was/08.html)

## USA and the Partition of Moscow Tartary

The American Declaration of Independence (1776) relative to Pugachev's defeat (1775) is not coincidental. The American colonies fought the [War of Independence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War) in the same years the last Hordian state collapsed.

> *"'The War of independence' in North America was the struggle with the weakening Russian Horde. The Romanovs attacked the Horde from the West — and from the East in America it was attacked by the Americans 'fighting for independence'."*
> — Fomenko, [Ch.8 §1](https://chronologia.org/en/how_it_was/08.html)

With Pugachev defeated, the Romanovs and the new United States divided Moscow Tartary: Russia took the Urals, Siberia, and Far East; Americans moved west. Alaska — the last Hordian territory in America — was transferred to the USA only in 1867. Hollywood's mythology of heroic frontiersmen versus savage Indians is the American cover story for the military conquest of the world's once-largest state.
