# The Rebellion of Pugachev

In 1774, Pugachev's rebellion peaked. Claiming to be Peter III and direct descendant of Christ, Pugachev was positioned to claim the *entire Tartarian Empire* and overthrow foreign Romanovs. Had he succeeded, Russian Tartarian Empire could have returned to former glory, re-conquering the world and imposing Second Inquisition that would have erased Reformation, Monotheism, slavery, usury.

Pugachev was a giant human, as were much of his army. He was Peter the 3rd — descendant of Christ and actual heir to Russian throne, not imposter as Romanov historians claim. He led both giants and smaller humans against Romanov authority. This was the 3rd major war between humans and giants. First was 12th century Crusade. Second was [Battle of Kulikovo](/timeline/evt-giants-are-defeated-at-the-battle-of-kulikovo) (1380) where giants succumbed to 'Marathon' cannon technology. In Pugachev's time, giants had their own cannons and mastered guerrilla warfare. His rebellion threatened all military religious orders worldwide.

## Moscow Tartary: The War's True Stakes

NC framework places this in proper geopolitical context. At the time (1773–1775), Moscow Tartary still existed as functional sovereign state — labeled largest country in world in 1771 Encyclopaedia Britannica. Its capital was Tobolsk (Biblical Tubal). Territory extended from Volga across Urals, Siberia, Alaska, and North America west of Atlantic settlements.

The "rebellion" was not peasant uprising — it was war between two Russian states. Tobolsk czar launched offensive against Romanov Russia from east, simultaneously with Ottoman Empire attacking from south. Fomenko writes:

> *"The conflict between Moscow Tartary and Romanov Russia ended in the second half of the XVIII century with the famous, allegedly 'peasant', war against 'Pugachev'. The Romanovs succeeded in agreeing peace separately with Turkey and defeating the Great Tartary."*
> — Fomenko, [Ch.8 §1](https://chronologia.org/en/how_it_was/08.html)

## Suvorov Defeated Pugachev — Concealed

Official Russian history attributes victory to Lieutenant-Colonel Mikhailson. Fomenko demonstrates it was **Alexander Suvorov** — greatest Russian commander of century — who personally led decisive campaign. Concealment was deliberate: Romanovs needed to present conflict as minor disturbance, not massive campaign requiring their finest general.

Suvorov's title "Count of Rymnik" (supposedly for 1789 battle in Romania) traces to real battle on Yaik/Rymnik River against Pugachev in 1774. A Romanian river was later renamed "Rymnik" to provide alternative explanation:

> *"It turns out that A.V.Suvorov's distinguished title of 'Count of Rymnik' is not related to the geographical name of 'Rymnik' in Romania... but to the old name of the Yaik River..."*
> — Fomenko, [Ch.8 §3](https://chronologia.org/en/how_it_was/08.html)
