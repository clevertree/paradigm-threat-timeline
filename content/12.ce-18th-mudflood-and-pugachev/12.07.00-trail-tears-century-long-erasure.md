# Trail Tears: Century-Long Erasure

The **Trail of Tears** is remembered as a forced relocation of Native Americans in the 1830s. But the real story begins **eighty-one years earlier**, in **1758**, with the establishment of the first Indian reservation in North America — the **Brotherton Indian Reservation** (Edgepillock) in New Jersey.

This predates the [MudFlood Energetic Event](/timeline/evt-the-mudflood-and-world-cataclysm) (~1774) by sixteen years. **The reservations did not follow the MFEE — they preceded it.** What followed was not a single march, but a **century-long campaign** to erase Native American civilization, memory, and technology.

---

## The Timeline: 1758 to 1851

### 1758: The First Reservation — Before the MFEE

The **Brotherton Indian Reservation** was established in **Burlington County, New Jersey**, in **1758** — the first formal reservation in North America. At this time, Native Americans still possessed:

- Advanced agricultural technology
- Sophisticated governance systems (Iroquois Confederacy influenced the U.S. Constitution)
- Complex trade networks spanning the continent
- Knowledge of metallurgy, earthworks, and large-scale construction

The reservation system **began the process of confinement** — forcing indigenous peoples onto restricted lands, severing trade routes, isolating communities.

---

### 1774: The MudFlood Energetic Event

Sixteen years after the first reservation, the [MudFlood Energetic Event](/timeline/evt-the-mudflood-and-world-cataclysm) devastates the planet. Cities are buried, populations vanish, entire regions are obliterated. The **Hordian world** — including remnants of the Rus-Horde Empire in North America — is reduced to wreckage.

Native Americans, already confined to reservations, are now **surrounded by a depopulated landscape**. The survivors of the MFEE — those who did not perish in the cataclysm — are scattered, their infrastructure destroyed, their knowledge fragmented.

---

### 1830–1839: The Official "Trail of Tears"

By 1830, the U.S. government formalizes what had been an ongoing process for **seventy-two years**. The **Indian Removal Act of 1830** mandates the relocation of the **Five Civilized Tribes** (Cherokee, Muscogee, Seminole, Chickasaw, Choctaw) to lands west of the Mississippi.

Between **1830 and 1839**, an estimated **60,000–100,000 Native Americans** are forcibly marched across **~5,045 miles**. Death toll: **13,200–16,700** (official estimate; likely higher).

But this is not the beginning. **It is the end.**

By 1839, Native Americans have been **confined, displaced, starved, and stripped of their technology** for **eighty-one years**. The 1830s relocation is the **final stage** of a process that began in 1758.

---

### 1851: Indian Appropriations Act — Institutionalizing the System

The **Indian Appropriations Act of 1851** officially initiates the **federal reservation system**, consolidating what had been an ad-hoc policy for nearly a century. By this point:

- Native Americans have lost their kingdoms
- Advanced technology is gone (or hidden/suppressed)
- The survivors are using "sticks and stones" — the image we inherit
- **408 Indian boarding schools** (1819–1969) enforce cultural erasure: "Kill the Indian, save the man"

---

## The Real Trail of Tears: A Century-Long Campaign

The **Trail of Tears** was not a single event. It was:

- **1758–1774:** Confinement to reservations, severing of trade networks, isolation
- **1774:** MFEE cataclysm destroys infrastructure, knowledge base, population centers
- **1774–1830:** Survivors hunted, relocated, displaced across a depopulated wasteland
- **1830–1839:** Official forced marches — the "Trail of Tears" as we know it
- **1839–1851:** Institutionalization of reservations, boarding schools, cultural genocide
- **1851–1969:** Federal reservation system enforces permanent confinement and erasure

**Total duration:** At least 93 years (1758–1851), arguably 211 (1758–1969). By 1839 the transformation was complete: Native Americans had lost their kingdoms, technology, and autonomy — confined to reservations, subjected to boarding schools, reduced to the "sticks and stones" narrative imposed by the erasure campaign.

## The Bering–Siberia Thesis (Speculative)

The strongest, most resistant populations may have been driven **far beyond Oklahoma**. Fomenko notes that **Siberia became an exile destination after Pugachev (1774)**. Timing suggests:

- **Before 1774:** Romanovs could not exile to Siberia — it was Moscow Tartary (Horde heartland)
- **After 1774:** Siberia is "wreckage" — depopulated, available as penal colony
- **After 1790:** First documented exile to Tobolsk (Radishev, 1790)

**Theory:** The most resistant Native American populations may have been driven across the Bering Strait into Siberia. Weaker groups remained on U.S. reservations. No primary-source evidence; timing suggestive.
