# Mars Literature: Author Profiles and Evidence

This appendix provides detailed author profiles and textual evidence supporting the argument in [Mars Literature as Reclassified Nonfiction](/timeline/evt-mars-literature-reclassified). Each entry documents what the author described, their institutional connections, and the investigative significance of their work.

For the overarching thesis — that pre-Wells Mars literature was nonfiction retroactively reclassified as science fiction — see the main article.

## Nonfiction Authors: The Astronomical Record

### Giovanni Schiaparelli (1835–1910)

Italian astronomer and director of the Brera Observatory in Milan. From 1877 onward, Schiaparelli mapped features on Mars he termed *canali* — Italian for "channels." The word was mistranslated into English as "canals," implying artificial construction. Whether Schiaparelli intended to suggest artificiality is debated, but his maps showed a geometric network of linear features connecting dark regions on the Martian surface. His observations were replicated by multiple astronomers and formed the empirical basis for Lowell's inhabited-Mars hypothesis.

### Camille Flammarion (1842–1925)

France's most prominent popular astronomer. His *La Planète Mars et ses conditions d'habitabilité* (1892) compiled **over 600 pages** of Mars observations from telescopic history and argued explicitly that Mars was inhabited by intelligent beings. Flammarion was not a fringe figure — he was president of the Société Astronomique de France and one of the most widely read science writers in Europe. His synthesis represented the mainstream scientific opinion of the 1890s.

### Percival Lowell (1855–1916)

American businessman turned astronomer who founded the Lowell Observatory in Flagstaff, Arizona, specifically to study Mars. Published three monographs — *Mars* (1895), *Mars and Its Canals* (1906), *Mars as the Abode of Life* (1908) — arguing that the canal network was artificial, built by an advanced civilisation managing scarce water across a drying planet. Mainstream science spent a century ridiculing Lowell. His observatory also led to the discovery of Pluto (1930). Lowell's canal observations, while disputed, were reported independently by other observers including William Henry Pickering and Andrew Ellicott Douglass.

### Alfred Russel Wallace (1823–1913)

Co-discoverer of natural selection. Wrote *Is Mars Habitable?* (1907) specifically to debunk Lowell's inhabited-Mars thesis. The question is not whether Wallace was right but **why the co-discoverer of evolution devoted an entire book to attacking a colleague's Mars observations**. Wallace was a naturalist with no expertise in planetary astronomy. The institutional urgency of his response — published the same year as Lowell's *Mars as the Abode of Life* — suggests coordinated opposition to the inhabited-Mars hypothesis at a level above individual scientific disagreement.

## Pre-Wells Fiction Authors (1880–1897)

### Percy Greg (1836–1889)

English writer. *Across the Zodiac: The Story of a Wrecked Record* (1880) is one of the earliest novels to describe space travel to Mars using a fictional propulsion technology ("apergy"). Greg describes Mars as inhabited by **humans** with an advanced civilisation, political intrigue, and a dictatorial theocracy. The novel includes detailed social and political structures — not the vague "alien civilisation" of later SF but specific institutions of governance and religious control. Greg died in 1889, nine years before Wells; his Mars is entirely pre-inversion.

### Robert Cromie (1855–1907)

Irish journalist and novelist. *A Plunge into Space* (1890) describes human-like Martians living in a **peaceful, utopian society**. Cromie's Mars has resolved the political and social problems that Earth still struggles with. The novel was published eight years before Wells and portrays Mars contact as mutually beneficial — the opposite of the Wells template.

### Gustavus W. Pope (1828–1902)

American physician and author. *Journey to Mars, the Wonderful World: Its Beauty and Splendor; Its Mighty Races and Kingdoms; Its Final Doom* (1894) describes human-like Martians with cities and technology. Pope is frequently cited as an influence on Edgar Rice Burroughs — his Mars features multiple races, advanced technology, and a specific social geography. He was a medical doctor, not a professional writer; his access to Mars descriptions may have come through channels unavailable to literary figures.

### Kurd Lasswitz (1848–1910)

German philosopher, mathematician, and author. *Auf zwei Planeten* (*Two Planets*, 1897) — published one year before Wells — describes Martians who are **superior but benign** and who establish their presence on Earth specifically at the **North Pole**. The polar station is the point of contact between the two civilisations. Lasswitz's Martians initially establish a benevolent protectorate over Earth, though political tensions eventually arise.

**Investigative significance:** Lasswitz places the Mars–Earth contact point at the North Pole — the same location where multiple esoteric, mythological, and anomalous traditions locate hidden civilisations or points of interdimensional contact. This German novel predates Wells by one year and remains far less known in the Anglophone world. A full analysis of Lasswitz's sources and the North Pole connection is warranted.

## Post-Wells Pulp Authors (1905–1960s)

### Garrett P. Serviss (1851–1929)

American astronomer and science journalist. *Edison's Conquest of Mars* (1898) was written as a direct response to Wells: humans build a fleet of spaceships and invade Mars in retaliation for the Martian attack. The critical detail: Serviss's Martians are revealed to have built the **Great Pyramid** and the **Sphinx** during a previous visit to Earth.

**Investigative significance:** The connection between Mars and the Egyptian pyramids — which Serviss introduced in the same year Wells published *War of the Worlds* — appears independently in multiple later traditions (ancient astronaut theory, Hoagland's *Monuments of Mars*, the "face on Mars" controversy). Serviss was a professional science journalist with access to the astronomical establishment. Where did the pyramid-Mars connection originate?

### Edwin Lester Arnold (1857–1935)

English author. *Lieut. Gullivar Jones: His Vacation* (1905) describes an Earth soldier transported to Mars where he has adventures among human-like Martians. Generally recognised as a direct precursor to Burroughs's Barsoom series. Arnold was the son of Sir Edwin Arnold, author of *The Light of Asia* — a family with connections to Eastern religious knowledge.

### Edgar Rice Burroughs (1875–1950)

Failed businessman who began writing Mars fiction in 1911 and published *A Princess of Mars* in 1912, followed by ten more Barsoom novels through 1943. **No known intelligence connections.** Burroughs had tried and failed at multiple careers — soldier, prospector, storekeeper, advertising man — before turning to fiction at age 36.

His eleven Barsoom novels contain the most detailed fictional account of Mars political structures in existence:
- **Mars is inhabited by humans.** The Red Martians are indistinguishable from Earth humans. Five races coexist — all humanoid.
- **Both poles are forbidden zones.** South: the Valley Dor and River Iss — a religious pilgrimage that is actually a trap run by the Holy Therns. North: the hidden kingdom of Okar, concealed behind a magnetic barrier.
- **Multi-layered conspiracy.** Each layer of the polar power structure believes it rules Mars while being exploited by the layer above.
- **Religious fraud as political control.** The entire planetary religion — the pilgrimage to the south pole — is a fabrication managed by a priestly caste for political power.

**Open question:** Where did Burroughs obtain this level of structural detail? The polar layered conspiracy, the religious-fraud-as-governance model, the specific racial geography — these are not generic adventure-fiction elements. Possible sources include Percival Lowell's observatory publications (Burroughs lived near Lowell Observatory in the American Southwest), Theosophical literature about Mars and planetary hierarchies, or oral traditions no longer traceable.

### Alexander Bogdanov (1873–1928)

Russian physician, philosopher, and senior Bolshevik who challenged Lenin on party organisation and was expelled. His novel *Red Star* (1908) describes a communist Mars with advanced technology, social harmony, and — critically — **advanced blood science**. Martians in *Red Star* use mutual blood transfusion as both medical treatment and a social sacrament that creates bonds between individuals.

Bogdanov later founded the Institute of Hematology in Moscow, dedicated to blood transfusion research. He died in 1928 during one of his own blood transfusion experiments — reportedly from a reaction after exchanging blood with a student who had malaria and tuberculosis.

**Investigative significance:** The correlation between his fictional subject (Martian blood science) and his cause of death (blood transfusion experiment) is either coincidence, a case of life imitating art, or something more sinister. Bogdanov was a political rival of Lenin who had been expelled from the Bolshevik inner circle — a dangerous position in 1920s Soviet politics.

### Alexei Tolstoy (1883–1945)

Russian author (distant relative of Leo Tolstoy). *Aelita* (1923) describes a Soviet engineer who travels to Mars and discovers a dying civilisation with a rigid caste system. He incites a workers' revolution against the Martian ruling class. The revolution fails.

**Investigative significance:** Written four years after the Russian Revolution. Tolstoy was a "Red Count" — an aristocrat who aligned with the Soviets. His Mars-revolution novel reads as the Russian Revolution replayed on another planet, with the critical difference that on Mars, the revolution **fails**. Whether this was political commentary, encoded knowledge, or literary exercise remains open.

### Stanley G. Weinbaum (1902–1935)

American science fiction author. *A Martian Odyssey* (1934) is considered a landmark story for portraying intelligent, friendly, non-humanoid Martians — one of the first "sympathetic alien" stories in American SF. Weinbaum died of lung cancer at age 33, just eighteen months after his first publication. His brief career produced some of the most influential alien-contact fiction in the genre.

### C.S. Lewis (1898–1963)

Oxford medievalist, literary scholar, and Christian apologist. His Space Trilogy — *Out of the Silent Planet* (1938), *Perelandra* (1943), *That Hideous Strength* (1945) — encodes pre-Copernican planetary cosmology as fiction. Lewis's Mars (Malacandra) is inhabited by three intelligent species — *hrossa*, *séroni*, *pfifltriggi* — all rational, moral, and civilised under the governance of Oyarsa, a planetary angel.

Lewis explicitly attacked Wells's materialist Mars in his fiction. Earth in his cosmology is the "Silent Planet" — **quarantined** from the rest of the solar system because its ruling angel fell. Mars is not the dangerous alien world of Wells; Earth is the problem.

**Investigative significance:** Lewis had deep knowledge of medieval planetary cosmology from his academic work. His Space Trilogy encodes the medieval model — planets as living beings governed by intelligences — within a fictional framework. He was a close friend and colleague of J.R.R. Tolkien at Oxford; their overlapping encoding projects suggest shared access to source material. For full analysis, see [Historical Antibodies: Detailed Profiles](/timeline/evt-antibodies-appendix).

### Leigh Brackett (1915–1978)

American author often called "the Queen of Space Opera." Her planetary romances — *The Sword of Rhiannon*, *Shadow Over Mars*, *The Secret of Sinharat*, and dozens of short stories — describe Mars as a dying but inhabited world with ancient human-like civilisations, lost cities, functional canals, and decadent ruling classes. Her Mars preserves the pre-Wells consensus intact: inhabited, ancient, beautiful, and decaying.

Brackett was married to Edmond Hamilton, another prolific Mars pulp writer. She also wrote the screenplay for *The Empire Strikes Back* (1980) and multiple Howard Hawks films (*The Big Sleep*, *Rio Bravo*, *El Dorado*). Her dual career in literary Mars fiction and Hollywood screenwriting places her at the intersection of both encoding channels — literature and film.

### Ray Bradbury (1920–2012)

American author. *The Martian Chronicles* (1950) describes telepathic, shape-shifting Martians who are "essentially human in emotion and culture" — beautiful, melancholy, and ultimately destroyed by human colonisation. They die from Earth diseases, the same bacterial motif that ended Wells's invasion. Bradbury acknowledged Burroughs as his primary influence.

**Investigative significance:** Bradbury's Mars inverts Wells's inversion — his colonisers are the villains, his Martians the victims. *The Martian Chronicles* encodes the destruction of an inhabited civilisation by colonisers, a narrative that maps onto the proposed [Mars Catastrophe](/timeline/evt-mars-catastrophe) read through the lens of Martian victims rather than Earth aggressors.

## Cold War and Late 20th Century Authors

### Robert Heinlein (1907–1988)

American author with **Naval intelligence background** — he worked at the Philadelphia Naval Yard during WWII (note the overlap with the [Philadelphia Experiment](/timeline/evt-philadelphia-experiment) timeline). His Mars fiction is notably more "realistic" and politically specific than pure pulp:

- *Red Planet* (1949): Mars has intelligent native Martians and human colonists; the colonists **revolt** against the colonial administration; the wise native Martians assist them.
- *Stranger in a Strange Land* (1961): Valentine Michael Smith, a human **raised by Martians**, returns to Earth with telepathic and psychokinetic powers. The telepathic capability encoded here appears independently across Wells, Bradbury, Dick, and multiple film productions.
- *Podkayne of Mars* (1963): A human girl raised on Mars as a colonist.

### Philip K. Dick (1928–1982)

American author who claimed to have experienced genuine psychic or mystical events (the VALIS experience, February–March 1974). His Mars fiction consistently features memory manipulation, hidden truth, and corporate conspiracy:

- *Martian Time-Slip* (1964): Mars colonists under corporate control; reality perception breaks down.
- *The Three Stigmata of Palmer Eldritch* (1965): Mars colonists use hallucinogenic drugs to escape their miserable reality; an entity from deep space offers a more powerful drug that may be a trap.
- *We Can Remember It for You Wholesale* (1966, adapted as *Total Recall* 1990): A man's real memories of Mars have been erased and replaced with false ones. A resistance movement on Mars fights against corporate tyranny. Alien technology hidden inside Mars could liberate the population if accessed.

**Investigative significance:** The *Total Recall* premise — your real memories of Mars have been erased and replaced — is itself a meta-description of the fiction-as-erasure technique. Dick may not have had intelligence access, but he recognised the operational pattern: memory erasure, manufactured reality, forbidden knowledge buried under corporate control.

### Kim Stanley Robinson (b. 1952)

American author with PhD in English. His Mars trilogy — *Red Mars* (1992), *Green Mars* (1993), *Blue Mars* (1996) — is the most politically sophisticated Mars-revolution narrative in the literature. The "First Hundred" colonists split into factions; Earth corporations attempt resource extraction; Mars declares independence; civil war ensues. Robinson incorporates real planetary science and a detailed political analysis of revolution, corporate control, and terraforming.

**Investigative significance:** Robinson's trilogy synthesises the entire prior Mars-literature tradition into a realistic political framework. The factional warfare, corporate exploitation, and independence revolution replicate the patterns laid down by Bogdanov, Tolstoy, Burroughs, and Heinlein — but updated for a post–Cold War audience. Whether Robinson drew consciously on the full tradition or arrived at the same structures independently is an open question.
