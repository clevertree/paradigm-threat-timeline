## 1894: Strange Light on Mars — Lick & Nice Observatories

A "great light" was observed on Mars's illuminated disc during the 1894 opposition — reported at Lick Observatory, confirmed by **Perrotin of Nice**. H.G. Wells opened his novel with this observation, interpreting it as *"the casting of the huge gun"* from which invasion cylinders were fired. Ten similar flashes followed over ten consecutive nights.

Whether Wells fictionalised an astronomical anomaly or disclosed an intelligence briefing, the observation is documented in the astronomical record.

## 1897: "The Crystal Egg" — Two-Way Surveillance Disclosure

Wells published "The Crystal Egg" in *The New Review* (May 1897), months before serialising *War of the Worlds*. The story describes a paired crystal device enabling **real-time two-way surveillance** between Earth and Mars. Through the crystal the observer sees: Martian architecture (circular openings, no doors, metallic tracery), winged Martians in flight, wingless large-headed Martians, white translucent bipeds (livestock?), and a **vast mechanism of shining metals**.

~20 crystal mast stations were observed on terraced Martian platforms. After the Earth-side crystal's owner died, it was purchased by *"a tall, dark man in grey"* and disappeared — intelligence recovery.

This is not fiction structure. It is a **surveillance technology briefing** formatted as a short story.

## 1897–1898: War of the Worlds — The Invasion Account

*War of the Worlds* was serialised in *Pearson's Magazine* (April–December 1897), published by Heinemann in January 1898. The narrative provides operationally precise detail: cylinder dimensions (~30 yards diameter), Heat-Ray characteristics (microwave-range directed energy matching the modern Active Denial System), Black Smoke deployment (chemical weapon), tripod assembly sequences, Martian physiology (bodiless brains dependent on machine infrastructure), and strategic objectives (*"complete demoralisation and the destruction of any opposition"* — not extermination).

## 1899: Tesla Detects Signals from Mars

At his Colorado Springs laboratory in 1899, Nikola Tesla observed unusual signals he speculated were communications from another planet. He wrote in a December 1899 letter and to the Red Cross Society in December 1900 that the signals appeared *"intelligently controlled."* In his February 1901 *Collier's Weekly* article "Talking With Planets," Tesla stated the signals could have come from Mars, Venus, or other planets.

The press jumped to "Mars." Tesla never retracted the claim. His Wardenclyffe Tower (1901–1905), funded by J.P. Morgan with $150,000, was ostensibly for transatlantic wireless — but Tesla's stated ambition was **interplanetary communication**. Morgan pulled funding; the tower was demolished.

Tesla also developed a "teleforce" beam weapon (1934) whose principles — open-ended vacuum tube, gas jet seal, tungsten slugs charged to millions of volts via electrostatic repulsion — bear structural resemblance to Wells's Heat-Ray. His DIA-adjacent contacts and the FBI/Office of Alien Property seizure of his papers within two days of his death (January 1943) suggest his work intersected classified Mars-related intelligence.

## 1942: Battle of Los Angeles — 1,400 Rounds at Unknown Targets

On February 24–25, 1942, the U.S. military fired **over 1,400 anti-aircraft rounds** at unidentified targets over Los Angeles. ONI had warned of an expected attack within 10 hours. Sirens sounded at 2:25 AM; firing continued 3:16–4:14 AM. **Five civilians died** (three from blackout car accidents, two from stress-induced heart attacks).

Key anomalies:
- **No bombs dropped.** No enemy aircraft downed. No wreckage recovered.
- Secretary of the Navy Frank Knox called it a **"false alarm"** the next day — contradicting the Army's insistence that real targets were present
- Japan confirmed post-war they flew **no aircraft over Los Angeles** that night
- The Air Force later attributed it to a **"weather balloon"** and war nerves — the same explanation used for Roswell five years later
- Contemporary photographs show searchlights converging on a luminous object

The incident occurred 10 weeks after Pearl Harbor. Whatever survived 1,400+ rounds without damage was not Japanese aircraft.

## 1947: Roswell — "Flying Disc" Announced, Then Retracted

In early July 1947, debris was recovered near **Corona, New Mexico.** On July 8, **Roswell Army Air Field** issued a press release announcing recovery of a "flying disc." The release was retracted within 24 hours, replaced with the claim of a **weather balloon.**

In 1994, the Air Force admitted the balloon story was cover — the debris was from **Project Mogul**, a classified nuclear test detection balloon. But the original press release exists: a military installation announced recovery of a non-terrestrial craft, then retracted it under pressure.

Key intelligence officer **Major Jesse Marcel**, who handled the debris, told Stanton Friedman in 1978 that the material was unlike anything terrestrial — thin metallic material that could not be dented or burned, with strange symbols. Marcel maintained this until his death.
