# Napoleon invades Russia — Reverse Crusade I (Extended)

Napoleon's invasion of Russia in 1812 — the [Grande Armée](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grande_Arm%C3%A9e)'s catastrophic six-month campaign across the same territories that had been Moscow Tartary just forty years earlier — is one of the most consequential military events in European history. The conventional narrative presents it as Napoleon's fatal overreach: an army of 680,000 that entered Russia and returned as fewer than 100,000 survivors.

We call it **Reverse Crusade I** — the first organized pan-European invasion of Russia. Per Fomenko, the original Crusades were launched *by* the Rus-Horde against the west. 1812 reversed the direction. See [Aftermath of 1812](/timeline/evt-aftermath-of-1812-russian-pursuit-to-paris) for the full Reverse Crusade framework.

## The Napoleon–Britain Question

Conventional historians present Napoleon as locked in a death struggle with Britain — the great naval rival he could never defeat. But examine what Napoleon actually *did*: he sold [Louisiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase) to Britain's own American colony in 1803, doubling the territory of the nascent United States. Britain's most important imperial offshoot received the heartland of North America — from a man supposedly at war with London. There was a major show of force across the Channel, just as in WW2, but if all of continental Europe genuinely wanted to conquer England, they could have done it, navy or no.

The question historians cannot answer: **why did Napoleon choose to invade Russia before finishing with England?** And why did Hitler make the identical choice 129 years later — turning east into Russia while Britain remained undefeated to his west? Two different leaders, two different centuries, same inexplicable strategic decision. The conventional explanation (madness, hubris, miscalculation) must be invoked *twice* for two separate men — or one recognizes the simpler answer: **neither Napoleon nor Hitler controlled their own campaigns.** Both were directed by the same financial network (centered on the City of London) whose actual target was always Russia — the last successor of the Horde. England was never the target because England housed the command structure.

## Why Moscow?

The invasion's strategic objective was forcing Czar Alexander I into negotiations by seizing the old symbolic capital. But Moscow in 1812 was no longer the center it had been. The Romanovs had relocated to St. Petersburg; Moscow was an administrative backwater. When Alexander refused to negotiate and the Russians burned Moscow themselves rather than let it serve as French winter headquarters, Napoleon had no strategic objective left. He occupied charred ruins for five weeks and retreated into the Russian winter.

In the NC framework, this has an additional dimension. The territories of former Moscow Tartary — Siberia, the Urals, the far east — had been incorporated into Romanov Russia only since the late 18th century. Local loyalty to the Romanov state was thin. A French-style liberation campaign promising feudal abolition might have found substantial sympathies among recently-conquered Hordian populations. The Romanov court was aware of this — explaining the ferocity of the scorched-earth response, designed to ensure Napoleon found no resources and no allies in the interior.

## The Battle of Borodino and Its Aftermath

The [Battle of Borodino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Borodino) (7 September 1812) was the largest single day's battle in the Napoleonic Wars — 70,000 casualties in one day. Neither side achieved a decisive outcome. The Russian army retreated but maintained cohesion. Napoleon occupied Moscow two weeks later. The subsequent French retreat became one of history's most devastating military disasters: cold, starvation, partisan attacks, and river crossings destroyed the army as an effective force.

## Why the Winter Was Unpredictable

The conventional explanation — that Napoleon was simply arrogant about logistics — misses two critical factors. First, the [MudFlood Energetic Event](/timeline/evt-the-mudflood-and-world-cataclysm) of ~1774 had destabilised Earth's climate barely forty years earlier. Weather patterns that had been reliable for centuries were now chaotic; the French high command planned based on historical data that was no longer valid. Second, whatever advanced technology the French possessed — the same systems that had made them militarily unstoppable across Europe — failed catastrophically in extreme cold. The superiority that had swept every coalition aside became useless in conditions the operators had never encountered. For the full theory on French technology and the war's deeper context, see [Aftermath of 1812](/timeline/evt-aftermath-of-1812-russian-pursuit-to-paris).

## Moscow Burns: The Population Decides

When the Russians burned Moscow before Napoleon's entry, the destruction was deliberate and total. The population and military leadership chose to **destroy their own most sacred structures** — including churches they considered their holy Jerusalem — rather than see them turned into brothels or desecrated by an occupying army with no understanding of Orthodox Hordian tradition. This was not merely a military tactic. It was a civilisational statement: a population that burns its own holy city will never submit to the force that drove them to it. Napoleon occupied charred ruins for five weeks and found neither allies nor resources.

## Napoleon and the Hordian Legacy

Tchaikovsky's [1812 Overture](/timeline/evt-tchaikovsky-releases-1812-overture), composed in 1880, celebrates the Russian victory. It opens with the Russian Orthodox hymn *God Preserve Thy People* (associated with the old Kievan/Hordian church tradition), transitions through French and Russian military themes, and culminates in the *Solemn March* with cannon fire and church bells. The NC reading sees the last musical celebration of the Hordian legacy: Orthodox bells, Old Russian hymns, and cannon fire all belonging to the cultural sphere the Romanovs had been systematically erasing since 1613 — temporarily recovered in the national trauma of 1812.

Napoleon's defeat did not restore the old order; it accelerated the financial one. The Russian army pursued Napoleon all the way to Paris — see [Aftermath of 1812](/timeline/evt-aftermath-of-1812-russian-pursuit-to-paris). With Napoleon removed, the Romanovs dominated the Eurasian heartland while the City of London dominated global finance. The stage was set for the 19th century's consolidation of the British Empire and the railroad-and-debt penetration of every remaining independent territory.
