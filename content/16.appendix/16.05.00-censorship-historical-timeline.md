# Censorship and Book Burning: The Historical Record

This article provides the detailed historical timeline supporting [Censorship and Book Burning: From Alexandria to the Internet](/timeline/evt-censorship-and-book-burning). For the pattern analysis and 21st century censorship, see that article.

## Ancient Record Destruction

### The Library of Alexandria

The destruction of the Library of Alexandria is the most famous case, but its framing as a single event obscures a more important truth: the library was **repeatedly** targeted across centuries, by multiple factions, precisely because it contained records that threatened successive power structures.

- **Caesar's siege fire** (48 BC, Scaligerian) — destruction of dockside warehouses, possibly including scroll repositories
- **Christian mob destruction** of the Serapeum (391 CE, Scaligerian) under Theophilus of Alexandria
- **Arab conquest** seizure (642 CE, Scaligerian) — attributed to Caliph Omar's alleged order

Each faction blamed the previous one. The end result: an irreplaceable collection of ancient astronomical, historical, and cosmological records — records that might have preserved pre-catastrophe knowledge — was completely eliminated.

Under Fomenko's analysis, these "separate" destructions may represent a single event duplicated across phantom centuries.

### Qin Shi Huang's Book Burning (213 BC, Scaligerian)

The first Emperor of unified China ordered the burning of all books that did not deal with agriculture, medicine, or divination — and specifically targeted historical records of rival states. Scholars who resisted were reportedly buried alive. The official justification: preventing the use of historical precedent to criticise the present dynasty.

### Roman Imperial Censorship

Roman authorities systematically destroyed texts deemed subversive, including Etruscan religious literature, Druidic oral traditions (suppressed through the destruction of the Druidic class itself in Gaul and Britain), and "sibylline" prophetic texts that were periodically purged and rewritten under state control.

## Medieval and Early Modern Destruction

### The Christianisation Campaigns

The conversion of Europe involved systematic destruction of pagan libraries, temple records, and oral traditions:

- **Monastic "preservation"** often meant **selective copying**. Texts deemed incompatible with Christian cosmology were simply not copied, ensuring their extinction when the original scrolls decayed.
- **The destruction of Mayan codices** by Bishop Diego de Landa (1562) — only four codices survived out of what was likely thousands. De Landa burned them specifically because they contained astronomical and historical records that contradicted the Biblical timeline.
- **The Inquisition's Index Librorum Prohibitorum** (1559–1966) — a continuously maintained list of forbidden books, enforced across Catholic Europe for over four centuries.

### The Reformation and Counter-Reformation

Both sides burned the other's books. The net effect: records from the pre-Reformation period were subjected to **double censorship**, with Catholics and Protestants each destroying texts that undermined their version of history.

## 18th–19th Century: Systematic Restructuring

### Scaliger and Petavius

Joseph Scaliger (1540–1609) and Dionysius Petavius (1583–1652) constructed the chronological framework that all subsequent Western historiography depends upon. As Fomenko's New Chronology demonstrates, this framework contains systematic errors and deliberate duplications that extend recorded history far beyond its actual span. The Scaligerian chronology was not a "discovery" — it was a **construction**, and it was enforced through the institutional authority of the Church and later the universities.

Any records that contradicted this chronology were not destroyed — they were **reinterpreted** within the Scaligerian framework, making their original meaning invisible.

### Napoleon's Egyptian Campaign and the Rosetta Stone

Napoleon's 1798 Egyptian campaign included extensive seizure of Egyptian records and artefacts. While the Rosetta Stone enabled the decipherment of hieroglyphics, the selection of which records to study, translate, and publish — and which to store in basements — has always been controlled by institutional gatekeepers.

## 20th Century: Industrial-Scale Destruction

The 20th century brought censorship to an industrial scale, with multiple overlapping campaigns:

### The Bolshevik Revolution (1917) and Soviet Censorship

The Bolsheviks did not merely censor — they **rewrote history in real time**. Soviet historiography (see related article) involved:

- Destruction of Tsarist-era archives and church records
- Physical elimination of the pre-revolutionary intellectual class (the 1922 "Philosophers' Steamer" deportations; see related article)
- Continuous revision of encyclopedias and reference works to reflect current Party doctrine
- The "memory hole" — Orwell's term, drawn from Soviet practice — whereby disgraced officials were literally erased from photographs and records

Lenin himself articulated the principle: *"All over the world, wherever there are capitalists, freedom of the press means freedom to buy up newspapers, to buy writers, to bribe, buy and fake 'public opinion' for the benefit of the bourgeoisie."*

### Nazi Book Burning (1933)

The *Bücherverbrennungen* of May 1933 are the iconic image of 20th century censorship. Student organisations, directed by the Propaganda Ministry, burned approximately 25,000 books in Berlin alone. Targeted works included those by Jewish authors, Marxists, pacifists, and — notably — sexologists and researchers whose work challenged conventional categories.

But the Nazi case also illustrates **selective preservation**: the regime maintained extensive private archives of seized material for its own research purposes. The destruction was public; the preservation was secret.

### Allied Bombing of German Archives (1943–1945)

The Allied strategic bombing campaign destroyed vast quantities of German historical archives, municipal records, and library collections. The destruction of the Preußische Staatsbibliothek and numerous city archives across Germany eliminated records stretching back centuries. Whether this was incidental to the bombing campaign or partly intentional remains debated.

### Operation Paperclip and the Seizure of Records (1945)

The American and Soviet seizure of German scientific, intelligence, and historical records after 1945 removed enormous quantities of material from public access. Much of this remains classified or restricted eight decades later.

### The Chinese Cultural Revolution (1966–1976)

Mao's Cultural Revolution resulted in the systematic destruction of temples, historical sites, classical texts, genealogical records, and cultural artefacts across China. The "Four Olds" campaign (old customs, old culture, old habits, old ideas) specifically targeted historical records and family lineage documents that connected the Chinese population to their pre-revolutionary past.

### Operation Mockingbird (1948–1975+)

The CIA's documented program to infiltrate and control media outlets represents a shift from **destruction** of records to **control of the narrative surrounding them**. Rather than burn books, you ensure that the books that get published, reviewed, and distributed support the approved version of events.
