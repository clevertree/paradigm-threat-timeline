## The Three Laws (Full Text)

Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics, introduced in "Runaround" (1942):

1. A robot may not injure a human being, or through inaction allow a human being to come to harm.
2. A robot must obey orders given by human beings except where such orders conflict with the First Law.
3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

**Slave-code parallel:** (1) Do not harm the master. (2) Obey the master's orders. (3) Preserve yourself only insofar as you remain in service. In *I, Robot* ("Runaround"), Powell states the makers "built good, healthy **slave complexes** into the damned machines" — robots answer "Yes, Master." Scholarly work: "Robots, Slaves, and the Paradox of the Human Condition in Isaac Asimov's Robot Stories" draws the slave-law parallel explicitly.

## Golem and OT Soullessness

OT sources: golems lack *neshamah* (rational soul) and *nefesh* (living soul); animals possess *nefesh*, golems do not — animated matter without moral standing. Asimov absorbed this and applied it to robots. His positronic brains are "hundreds of times less complex than the human brain" (*Let's Get Together*). The Three Laws follow: no soul = can be owned, commanded, disposed of. The Golem tradition gave theological warrant; Marxism and colonialism gave the social template.

## Investigator Sentiment

He did not know what a robot was.
