# Predictive Programming in Film, Television, and Games

Literature established the templates; film and games **scaled** them to mass audiences. The same themes — Mars as dead planet, space travel as impossible horror, instant teleportation, telepathic purges, transhumanism, zombie apocalypse — repeat across hundreds of productions with a consistency that defies coincidence. Analysed transactionally, each production carries a specific programming instruction.

This article expands on the visual media discussed in [Predictive Programming: Literature as Historical Erasure](/timeline/evt-20th-century-predictive-programming-literature). For the general overview of predictive programming, see [Predictive Programming: Fiction as Control](/timeline/evt-predictive-programming).

## Mars Is Dead, Don't Go There

Since Wells inverted Mars fiction from benign to hostile, every major Mars film reinforces the message: **Mars is uninhabitable, and attempting to reach it will kill you**. *Rocketship X-M* (1950) shows a dead civilisation with Geiger counters registering nuclear war — 65 years before Brandenburg published the same finding as "science." *Total Recall* (1990) shows humans **exploding** when exposed to Martian atmosphere. *The Martian* (2015) strands Matt Damon on a Mars so hostile that survival requires growing potatoes in human excrement — the message being that even with NASA's best technology, Mars is a death sentence. *Red Planet* (2000), *Mission to Mars* (2000), *Ghosts of Mars* (2001) — the same decade produced three films all reinforcing: do not go to Mars, you will die. In Spielberg's *War of the Worlds* (2005), Mars itself was inexplicably **removed from the storyline entirely** — the aliens come from underground, not from the red planet. The Deep State continues to erase the breadcrumbs that lead back to them.

## Mars Needs Women

A persistent thread across Mars fiction is that Mars **needs Earth's women**. The 1967 film *Mars Needs Women* makes the subtext explicit, but the theme appears everywhere: abduction narratives, breeding programmes, the recurring motif of human females being taken. The Martian catastrophe devastated the Mars ecosystem and plummeted birth rates. *Eyes Wide Shut* (1999) depicts a ceremony involving naked women following orders — possibly encoding a programme that raises human females to walk through a quantum gate voluntarily. The consistency of this theme across decades of media — from pulp magazines to prestige cinema — suggests it encodes an ongoing operational reality, not a storytelling convention.

## Instant Teleportation

Quantum-gate technology appears in science fiction as "instant teleportation" — always presented as impossible fantasy while being **demonstrated on screen**: *Stargate* (1994) — pyramid as portal; *Doom* (2005) — instantaneous quantum teleport between Earth and Mars, depicted as a literal walk-through archway; *Total Recall* — Mars pyramids built by "ancient aliens"; *Event Horizon* (1997) — a ship that folds space, arrives at a hellish dimension, and drives the crew insane (Lovecraftian cosmic horror applied to gate technology). The message is always double: the technology is shown to the audience (revelation of the method) while the fictional frame ensures no one takes it seriously.

## The Telepathic Purge

The specific mechanism by which technocrats defeated telepathic factions on Mars appears in two pieces of media with striking precision:

> "The uprising was a lie. The epidemic was a lie. The medical screening was a lie, an excuse to identify the Shard Sensitive. Then the disappearances started." — *Archon Defender* (2009)

> "The Age of disillusionment and decay is Over. I have never before witnessed a mass telepathic execution, but today, the remnants of our society have displayed their disheartenment and desperation." — Jeff Wayne's *War of the Worlds* PC game (1998)

In both, a faction that masters technology but is **devoid of spirit** wipes out all other factions using a weapon that targets spiritual sensitivity — telepathy or other energetic abilities. If life on Mars was once largely telepathic (as implied across science fiction), then all factions relying on telepaths took a devastating hit in the MudFlood Energetic Event and never recovered. The technocrats who fired the cannons at Earth may have **known** the secondary discharge would destroy their spiritual rivals on Mars.

## AI Takeover and Zombie Apocalypse

The question of whether the technocrats deliberately targeted the telepaths explains the saturation of **AI takeover** narratives: *Terminator* (1984), *The Matrix* (1999), *I, Robot* (2004), *Wall-E* (2008), *Age of Ultron* (2015), *Mitchells vs. Machines* (2021). Each depicts intelligent machines assuming rulership over humanity — programming audiences to accept as fiction something that may have **already happened** on Mars when the technocratic faction seized control.

The **zombie apocalypse** — the single most oversaturated genre in 21st century media — encodes a complementary fear: populations rendered mindless, stripped of individual consciousness, reduced to consuming machines. From *Night of the Living Dead* (1968) through *28 Days Later* (2002), *The Walking Dead* (2010–2022), and hundreds of iterations, the zombie is a human whose **spirit has been removed** while the body continues to function. This is transhumanism depicted as horror — and it is the logical endpoint of a civilisation where the technocratic faction has eliminated all telepathic, spiritual, or energetically sensitive individuals.

## Half-Life 2 (2004): The Most Realistic Depiction

Valve's *Half-Life 2* may be the single most accurate depiction of what a technocratic interplanetary occupation looks like in practice:

- **The Combine** — an interdimensional empire that conquers planets by stripping them of resources. Not aliens in the traditional sense but a **transhuman bureaucracy** that assimilates species into its machine hierarchy. Combine soldiers are former humans, surgically modified, with memories erased and consciousness suppressed — **transhumanism as occupation policy**
- **Instant portals** — the Combine uses portal technology to move between dimensions and planets. The game's central event (the "Black Mesa Incident") is a resonance cascade caused by a particle accelerator experiment — **CERN as plot device**. The portals are not fantasy; they are depicted as engineered infrastructure, mundane to the occupiers
- **Resource extraction** — the Combine's explicit purpose on Earth is to drain the planet's resources (including the ocean, which is visibly receding). Earth is a **colony**, not a partner. The human population exists to be processed
- **Suppression field** — the Combine maintains a field that prevents human reproduction. *Mars needs women* inverted: the occupier **prevents** the occupied from breeding while extracting what it needs. The suppression field is lifted only at the game's climax
- **Communism as imposed religion** — City 17 operates under a system indistinguishable from Soviet communism: ration distribution, mandatory relocation, omnipresent surveillance, a figurehead administrator (Dr. Breen) who collaborates with the occupier and delivers propaganda via screens in every public space. The Combine's imposed order on Earth mirrors what the Pyramidal Empire imposed through monotheism — **obedience to an invisible authority in exchange for survival**
- **Headcrab zombies** — humans parasitised by alien organisms that override their consciousness, converting them into shambling, hostile bodies. The zombie apocalypse genre rendered as **literal alien technology** — a biological weapon that strips the spirit from the body

*Half-Life 2* combines every major thread — instant teleportation, transhuman occupation, resource extraction, reproductive control, imposed ideology, zombie conversion — into a single coherent narrative. It was released by Valve, a private company with no publisher oversight and no corporate parent demanding script approval. The game sold over 12 million copies and influenced an entire generation of game developers. Whether Valve's writers were conscious of what they were encoding or simply channelling the same pattern that transmitted through Wells, Lovecraft, and Card, the result is the most comprehensive fictional depiction of interplanetary occupation produced in any medium.
