# Investigative Strategy

This document defines how the Paradigm Threat Timeline approaches investigations. It applies to all research in `investigations/`, to sourcing for timeline articles, and to how we treat claims that conflict with mainstream consensus.

---

## Core Principle: Objective from Scratch

We treat every possibility as **open until the weight of evidence and reasoning closes it** — and we do not treat "scientific consensus" or "lack of evidence" as sufficient to close it.

- **Start from zero.** Assume any possibility can be true. Our job is to trace claims, weigh sources, and document what we find — not to pre-judge by consensus or institutional authority.
- **We are not ruling things out** because:
  - "Science says so" (consensus is a social fact, not proof)
  - "There is no evidence" (absence of public evidence may reflect classification, gatekeeping, or redaction; see Burden of Proof below)
  - "That's a conspiracy theory" (the label is often used to stop inquiry; we document the claim and the counter-claim)

---

## Evidence and "Lack of Evidence"

### When There Is No "Evidence" but Plenty of Media

Sometimes there is no accepted physical or documentary evidence for a claim — but there *is* sustained media, entertainment, or narrative framing around it. We treat that as **relevant data**, not as proof of the claim.

- **Predictive programming:** If the same scenario (e.g. "portal opened at a lab," "dimensions," "something came through") appears repeatedly in film, news headlines, and viral posts, we note it. Predictive programming can mean: (1) disclosure in disguise, (2) inoculation so that if it happens the public dismisses it, or (3) coincidence. We do not assume (1), but we do not dismiss it because "it's just fiction" or "it's been fact-checked."
- **Fact-checkers:** Fact-checks that conclude "false" or "misunderstood" are one source among many. They often rest on "official says no" or "no peer-reviewed proof." Under this strategy, "official says no" does not by itself close the question; we record what officials said and what others claimed, and leave the interpretation open where appropriate.
- **Viral or "conspiracy" framing:** Claims that go viral or get labelled conspiracy theories are documented with the same care as mainstream claims. We trace primary sources (e.g. a CERN director's own words) and distinguish between what was actually said and what was later "clarified" or denied.

### When the Old Belief Was the Default

Many things that humans widely believed **before the 19th century** were later overturned by a new scientific or institutional narrative (e.g. the aether, a living cosmos, the possibility of non-material consciousness).

- **Burden of proof on the new scientists.** When the default position for centuries was "X is true" (e.g. the aether exists; waves need a medium), the burden of proof lies with those who say "X is false" or "X is meaningless." It is not enough to say "we have a new model that doesn't use X"; the question is whether the old framework was *disproven* or merely *replaced* by convention.
- **Replacement vs. disproof.** Replacing the aether with "quantum vacuum" or "Higgs field" is a change of name and model, not necessarily a disproof of "a medium exists." We note when modern physics reintroduces aether-like concepts under new names and treat that as continuity, not refutation.
- **Secrecy and burden.** If the institutions that overturned the old view later operated under heavy secrecy (e.g. military classification, proprietary research), the burden of proof has not been discharged in the public domain. We record that the public cannot verify the new consensus and that the burden remains on the overturning party.

## How to Use This in Investigations

1. **State the claim clearly** — including "fringe" or "conspiracy" claims — and trace them to primary sources where possible.
2. **State the mainstream or official position** — without treating it as the default truth. Record what was said, by whom, and when.
3. **Note predictive programming and media patterns** — repeated themes in film, news, social media — as data, not as proof or disproof.
4. **Apply burden of proof** — When the claim is "the old belief was wrong," ask: who bore the burden? Was it met in open, replicable form? Or was it met only inside closed institutions?
5. **Leave interpretation open when unsure** — Use "open question," "unresolved," or "see OUTSTANDING_QUESTIONS" rather than asserting closure. When in doubt, ask the project owner for interpretation rather than defaulting to consensus.

## Summary

| We do | We don't |
|-------|----------|
| Treat any possibility as admissible until evidence and reasoning close it | Rule out claims because of "scientific consensus" or "lack of evidence" alone |
| Use predictive programming and media patterns as data | Dismiss narratives as "just fiction" or "fact-checked false" without tracing primary sources |
| Put burden of proof on those who overturn long-standing belief | Assume the new scientists have met the burden when verification is secret or institutional |
| Document official and unofficial claims; distinguish primary sources from spin | Treat "official says no" or "fact-check: false" as the end of the inquiry |
| Ask for interpretation when unsure | Default to mainstream conclusion when evidence is ambiguous |
